## Topic

Strategy Analysis: Current State

## Key Concepts

### Strategy Analysis and Current State

Strategy analysis examines how a business need can be addressed through change. Current state analysis is the first part of that work because it explains how the organisation currently operates in relation to the need.

Current state analysis asks:

- Why does the business need to change some aspect of how it operates?
- What is currently not working, inefficient, risky, or misaligned?
- What would be directly or indirectly affected by the change?
- What evidence from elicitation supports the description of the current state?

The current state description should not be a general history of the organisation. It should be a focused explanation of the current situation that matters for the business need.

### Inputs and Outputs

| Element | Role in current state analysis |
| --- | --- |
| Business needs | Inputs that explain the problem or opportunity being investigated. |
| Confirmed elicitation results | Evidence gathered through interviews, workshops, surveys, document analysis, observation, or other elicitation techniques. |
| Current state description | Output that explains what is happening now and why change may be needed. |
| Business requirements | Output that states the high-level goals, objectives, or outcomes required to address the business need. |

The lecture distinguishes business needs from business requirements:

| Concept | Question answered | Example pattern |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Business need | Why is change needed? | Reduce IT costs; improve customer feedback; increase automation. |
| Business requirement | What needs to be achieved at a high level? | Merge IT systems; improve customer support processes; replace manual activities. |

Business needs are usually inputs into current state analysis. Business requirements are developed from the analysis of the need and current state.

### Sources of Business Needs

Business needs can arise from different levels or drivers:

| Source | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Top-down | A strategic goal from senior leadership creates a need for change. |
| Bottom-up | Operational workers or users identify a problem in current processes, systems, or work practices. |
| Middle management | Managers need better information, functions, or coordination to meet business objectives. |
| External drivers | Customers, competitors, regulation, markets, or technology changes create pressure to respond. |

The source matters because it shapes who should be involved, what evidence should be gathered, and how the need should be framed.

### POPIT and Current State Description

The lecture uses POPIT to structure current state analysis:

| POPIT area | Current state focus |
| --- | --- |
| Organisation | Business model, organisational structure, management structure, roles, responsibilities, and strategic alignment. |
| Processes | Activities used to create value through products or services. |
| Information and Technology | Systems, data, digital tools, infrastructure, and technology support for work and decision making. |
| People | Skills, competencies, job design, experience, culture, workload, and stakeholder expectations. |

POPIT helps the analyst avoid treating the current state as only a technology problem. A business need may involve systems, but the underlying current state may also involve people, process, organisational design, or the relationships between these areas.

The current state description should distil raw elicitation data into a concise account. The analyst acts as a filter: evidence is included when it helps explain the business need, and irrelevant background detail is left out.

### Personas

A persona is a fictional representation of a target customer or user segment based on research. It represents an average or archetypal person from a larger group, not a real individual.

Personas help analysts:

- Think from the perspective of customers or users rather than from the perspective of designers or developers.
- Communicate how groups of users behave, think, and make decisions.
- Understand what users want to accomplish and why.
- Identify goals, frustrations, motivations, abilities, and contexts.
- Prioritise functions based on user needs.

Personas can be used for external customers or internal users of a digital solution.

### Creating Personas

Personas should be based on research rather than imagination.

Typical process:

1. Collect information through surveys, interviews, questionnaires, analytics, observation, or other research.
2. Group customers or users into segments with similar needs, behaviours, and attitudes.
3. Synthesize characteristics and define goals.
4. Check for completeness and redundancy so personas are distinct rather than overlapping.
5. Designate persona types, such as primary personas.
6. Expand the description of attributes and behaviours.

Common persona information includes:

| Area | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Who | Name, age, gender, education, job, location, and other demographic details. |
| Context | Where, when, and in what environment the product or service is used. |
| Activities | What the user does, how often, and at what volume. |
| Attitudes | How the user thinks about the product domain and technology. |
| Aptitudes | Education, training, learning ability, and confidence. |
| Motivations | Why the user is engaged and what outcome they want. |
| Skills | Capabilities related to the product domain or technology. |

The aim is not to create a decorative profile. The aim is to make user needs concrete enough to guide analysis, prioritisation, and design.

### Customer Journey Maps

A customer journey map is a visual representation of the experiences a customer or user has with an organisation, product, service, or system. It shows the journey from the customer's perspective.

Customer journey maps help analysts:

- Capture interactions between customers and the organisation.
- Understand the process a customer goes through to achieve a goal.
- Identify touchpoints and channels.
- Analyse user experience and emotional responses.
- Uncover moments of frustration or pain.
- Reveal opportunities to improve the product, service, process, or digital solution.

In digital business analysis, customer journey maps are useful because customer value often depends on the whole experience, not only on internal process efficiency.

### Components of a Customer Journey Map

| Component | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Actor | The customer, user, or persona whose journey is being mapped. |
| Scenario and expectation | The goal or situation being analysed. |
| Journey phases | Stages of the customer or user experience. |
| Touchpoints | Points where the customer interacts with the organisation, product, service, channel, or system. |
| Actions | What the customer does at each stage. |
| Mindsets and emotions | What the customer thinks and feels during the journey. |
| Opportunities | Pain points or improvement areas revealed by the journey. |

The simple formula "do, think, feel" can be used to structure each stage of the journey.

### Creating Customer Journey Maps

A typical process is:

1. Identify the customer or user, often through a persona.
2. Understand the customer's goal.
3. Identify journey phases and touchpoints.
4. Describe how and where the customer interacts with the product, service, organisation, or solution.
5. Identify pain points and opportunities.
6. Represent the journey visually.

The right level of detail matters. Too little detail hides important pain points; too much detail can make the journey hard to understand.

### AI Support for BA Techniques

The lecture notes that generative AI can support outputs such as personas, current state descriptions, and customer journey maps, but generic foundation-model output is likely to be average unless the tool is given context.

For BA work, AI support is more useful when the analyst provides:

- Relevant business context.
- Existing examples or templates.
- Elicitation results.
- Clear instructions about required structure and quality.
- The purpose of the analysis output.

The analyst still remains responsible for evidence, judgement, relevance, and validation with stakeholders.

## Textbook Connections

Milani Chapter 8 connects directly to the lecture's focus on current state analysis, business needs, scope, products and services, personas, and customer journey maps.

### Business Needs

Milani defines business needs as strategically important problems or opportunities. The need may be strategic for the whole organisation or for a specific department, function, product, or process.

Important points from Milani:

- The business need guides what solutions can reasonably be considered.
- A solution should satisfy the organisation's need and create organisational value, not merely reflect one stakeholder's preference.
- Needs can arise top-down, bottom-up, from middle management, or from external drivers.
- Stakeholders may describe a solution when they are really trying to express a need.
- The analyst must redirect solution-focused conversations back to the underlying problem, adverse impact, consequences, and value.

This supports the lecture's distinction between business needs as the "why" and business requirements as high-level statements of what must be achieved.

### Current State Analysis

Milani describes current state analysis as the baseline for change. It clarifies why change is needed and what parts of the current solution, organisation, process, product, service, information system, or performance context are affected.

The chapter also stresses that business analysis is iterative. Analysts may move between needs, current state, future state, and solution ideas as new information emerges. This matches the lecture's point that the analyst distils evidence into a concise current state description rather than mechanically recording all raw data.

### Scope

Milani explains that scope is refined as the business need becomes clearer. Scope helps define:

- What is included.
- What is excluded.
- Where analysis effort should be focused.
- Which processes, products, systems, markets, capabilities, or situations are affected.

This links Week 8 current state analysis to the scope material from earlier lectures and to future state analysis in the next lecture.

### Products, Services, Personas, and Journey Maps

Milani connects products and services to value propositions. In the digital era, products and services may include physical components, smart components, connectivity, data, subscriptions, digital interfaces, and attached services.

Personas and customer journey maps help keep that value proposition user-centred:

- Personas give a research-based view of customer or user segments.
- Personas should be specific, grounded in evidence, and distinct from each other.
- Customer journey maps complement company-centred models by showing the process from the customer's perspective.
- Journey maps can reveal dissatisfaction, friction, channel switching, delays, and improvement opportunities.
- Digital analytics can strengthen journey maps by using evidence from real interactions rather than only stakeholder assumptions.

## Summary

- Current state analysis explains how the organisation currently operates in relation to a business need.
- Business needs are the "why" of change; business requirements are high-level statements of what must be achieved.
- Business needs can arise top-down, bottom-up, from middle management, or from external drivers.
- POPIT structures current state analysis across organisation, processes, information and technology, and people.
- Personas represent research-based customer or user segments and help analysts keep design and requirements user-centred.
- Customer journey maps show the customer or user experience across touchpoints and help identify pain points and opportunities.
- Milani Chapter 8 reinforces that strategy analysis matures value from an initial idea into clearer needs, scope, current state understanding, and user-centred analysis.
