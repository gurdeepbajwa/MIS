## Topic

Introduction to Business Analysis

## Key Concepts

### Business Analysis Core Concepts (BACCO)

| Concept | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Change | The act of transformation in response to a need. |
| Need | A problem or opportunity to be addressed. |
| Solution | A specific way of satisfying one or more needs in a context. |
| Stakeholder | A group or individual with a relationship to the change, the need, or the solution. |
| Value | The worth, importance, or usefulness of something to a stakeholder within a context. |
| Context | The circumstances that influence, are influenced by, and provide an understanding of the change. |

These concepts help frame business analysis work by connecting the reason for change, the people affected, the proposed response, and the value expected from it.

### Business Analysis Knowledge Areas and Competencies

The lecture connects business analysis to the BABOK view of the discipline. Business analysis is not only a job title; it is a set of practices and competencies used to understand needs, enable change, and support value delivery.

Core knowledge areas include:

- Business analysis planning and monitoring.
- Elicitation and collaboration.
- Requirements life cycle management.
- Strategy analysis.
- Requirements analysis and design definition.
- Solution evaluation.

These knowledge areas are supported by transferable underlying competencies such as analytical thinking, problem solving, communication, facilitation, stakeholder engagement, business knowledge, and technology awareness. The analyst uses these competencies to translate between business concerns and technical possibilities.

Frameworks, techniques, and models act as mental scaffolding for analysis. They may not always be used visibly or formally in every meeting, but they help the analyst structure questions, compare options, and produce clearer outcomes.

## Summary

- Successful digitalisation initiatives require strong alignment between business needs and technology capabilities, which can be difficult to achieve.
- Digital business analysis is a disciplined approach to introducing technological solutions that address business needs and support effective organisational change.
- Business analysts need a strong understanding of both business and technology so they can bridge the communication gap between business and IT stakeholders.
- Business analysis now plays a broader role in the digital age and is actively involved across many aspects of business.
- Business analysis capability remains useful across different roles because the discipline is grounded in transferable analysis, communication, and change-enablement skills.

## Notes

### Reading: Milani Chapter 1

Milani Chapter 1 introduces business analysis as a profession, explains why it became important, and shows how the role has expanded in the digital era. The chapter frames business analysis as work that helps organisations understand needs, recommend valuable solutions, and manage change in context.

#### Origins of Business Analysis

- Business analysis grew alongside the increased use of information systems in business, especially from the 1980s onward.
- Many IT projects failed because they exceeded time or budget, changed repeatedly, or did not deliver expected business value.
- A key early problem was weak communication between business stakeholders and IT developers.
- The business analyst role developed to translate business needs into requirements and help ensure that technology work addressed the right problems.
- The role has since expanded beyond requirements documentation into analysing business drivers, goals, change impacts, stakeholders, and value.

#### What Business Analysis Does

- Business analysis is concerned with enabling change by defining needs and recommending solutions that deliver value to stakeholders.
- The work can occur at strategic, tactical, operational, project, or continuous-improvement levels.
- Business analysis is often connected to information systems change, but it is not limited to software delivery.
- The central task is to understand the real problem or opportunity before recommending a solution.
- A good solution must fit the business context; technology can increase value when aligned with business needs, but it can also amplify problems if the process or problem definition is poor.

#### Business Analysis Core Concept Model

Milani uses the Business Analysis Core Concept Model to explain the recurring concepts in business analysis:

| Concept | Study meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Change | Transformation introduced in response to a need. |
| Need | The problem or opportunity that motivates analysis. |
| Solution | The proposed way of satisfying the need. |
| Stakeholder | People or groups affected by, involved in, or interested in the change. |
| Value | The worth or usefulness created for stakeholders. |
| Context | The internal and external circumstances that shape the need, solution, and value. |

These concepts are interdependent. If one changes, the analyst should reconsider the others. For example, a changed stakeholder group may alter what counts as value, which may require a different solution.

#### BABOK and Knowledge Areas

- Milani positions BABOK as a major body of knowledge for business analysis.
- BABOK organises business analysis work into knowledge areas such as planning and monitoring, elicitation and collaboration, requirements life cycle management, strategy analysis, requirements analysis and design definition, and solution evaluation.
- The knowledge areas are not a strict step-by-step method. They are applied iteratively depending on the project context.
- Elicitation, collaboration, and requirements management occur throughout the whole business analysis process.

#### Business Analysis Process

Milani presents business analysis as a flexible process rather than a rigid sequence:

1. Understand the business context.
2. Initiate and plan the business analysis work.
3. Analyse the current state and clarify the real problem, need, or opportunity.
4. Define the desired future state.
5. Assess and select a suitable solution.
6. Design the solution and document requirements.
7. Support delivery or implementation.
8. Evaluate whether the solution delivers the intended value.

The process may be short and informal for small problems, or long and iterative for complex organisational change.

#### Digital Era

- Digitalisation changes the context in which business analysis occurs by creating new ways to use data, information systems, platforms, automation, and digital infrastructure.
- Digital business analysis requires attention to business models, customer value, process change, and technology capabilities.
- The digital business analyst needs enough technology understanding to see what digital tools can enable, and enough business understanding to judge whether those tools create real value.
- Digital technologies should not be treated as solutions by default. They must be assessed against business needs, stakeholder value, feasibility, and organisational fit.

#### Development and Maturity of Business Analysis

- Business analysis maturity can develop from basic awareness to more structured, enterprise-wide, and strategically integrated practice.
- At lower maturity, business analysis may be project-focused and centred on requirements.
- At higher maturity, business analysts help align projects with business strategy, manage portfolios of change, develop business cases, and support competitive improvement.
- As analysts become more senior, they tend to move from narrow requirements work toward broader strategic and organisational analysis.

#### Role of the Business Analyst

Milani describes the business analyst as someone who investigates business situations, identifies needs and causes, evaluates improvement options, defines requirements, and helps ensure solutions are implemented in line with business needs.

The role includes several perspectives:

- Analyst: investigates problems, identifies causes, and understands systems.
- Facilitator: supports stakeholder discussion, elicitation, and shared understanding.
- Negotiator: helps stakeholders resolve disagreement and build consensus.
- Architect: contributes to solution, process, data, and organisational design.
- Planner: organises business analysis activities and manages requirements work.

The business analyst's role changes across the process. Early work is more investigative, middle stages involve negotiation and evaluation, and delivery stages involve supporting implementation and checking alignment with business needs.

#### Competencies of a Business Analyst

Important competencies include:

- Analytical thinking and problem solving.
- Learning and absorbing information from different sources.
- Conceptual and holistic thinking, especially where people, processes, data, and technologies interact.
- Visual communication and modelling to make complex situations easier to discuss.
- Creative thinking to identify better alternatives.
- Decision making and prioritisation.
- Organisation and time management.
- Communication, listening, facilitation, and stakeholder interaction.
- Adaptability when conditions, scope, methods, or stakeholder needs change.

#### Business and Technology Knowledge

- Business analysts need external business knowledge, such as industry trends, common practices, regulations, and how similar problems have been solved elsewhere.
- They also need internal business knowledge, such as organisational structure, strategy, business model, informal power, terminology, and decision pathways.
- Technology knowledge is important because most modern business improvements involve information systems.
- Analysts should understand digital capabilities such as data-driven decision-making, SaaS, mobile technologies, automation, and other information systems trends well enough to judge their relevance.

Connection to lecture:

- The BACCO concepts from the lecture provide a simple way to frame the chapter: a business analyst investigates a need in context, works with stakeholders, considers possible solutions, and assesses whether the change creates value.
- The lecture summary reinforces Milani's point that successful digitalisation depends on alignment between business needs and technology capabilities.
