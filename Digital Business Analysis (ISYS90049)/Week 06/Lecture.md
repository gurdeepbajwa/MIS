## Topic

Digital Business Analysis in Practice

## Key Concepts

### Business Analysis as a Professional Capability

The lecture frames business analysis as a professional capability rather than only a job title. A person may work under titles such as business analyst, product owner, consultant, solution analyst, manager, or delivery lead, but still rely on business analysis capabilities.

Business analysis capability includes:

- Understanding business problems and opportunities.
- Clarifying needs before solutions are chosen.
- Engaging stakeholders with different priorities and levels of authority.
- Translating business concerns into analysis outputs that can guide design and delivery.
- Supporting decisions about value, feasibility, risk, and change.

This means BA skills remain useful as roles change. Senior roles may involve less hands-on requirements documentation, but they still require analysis, stakeholder communication, judgement, and value-focused decision making.

### Requirements Are Elicited, Not Handed Over

The lecture emphasises that requirements are rarely simply "given" to the analyst. Stakeholders often describe what they want, what frustrates them, or what solution they assume is needed. The business analyst must investigate the underlying need.

Useful BA behaviour includes:

- Asking why a request matters.
- Separating wants from needs.
- Looking for patterns across different stakeholder views.
- Identifying where a request is a symptom of a deeper problem.
- Translating stakeholder language into requirements, models, options, or decision material.

This connects to elicitation because the analyst draws out knowledge that stakeholders may not have already structured as requirements.

### End-to-End BA Contribution

In practice, business analysis can contribute across the whole change initiative:

1. Ideation: helping sponsors and leaders test whether an idea is legitimate, valuable, and worth investigating.
2. Problem and opportunity analysis: clarifying the current situation, drivers, and business value.
3. Option assessment: comparing possible ways to address the need.
4. Planning and approach: shaping how analysis and delivery work should proceed.
5. Requirements and design support: defining, analysing, validating, and communicating requirements.
6. Delivery support: helping teams interpret requirements and make trade-offs.
7. Solution validation: checking whether the delivered solution is fit for purpose and aligned with the need.

This reinforces the course theme that BA work is broader than documenting requirements.

### Predictive, Adaptive, and Hybrid Practice

In practice, the chosen approach depends on uncertainty and complexity.

| Approach | Practical use |
| --- | --- |
| Predictive | Useful when the problem, requirements, solution direction, and delivery pathway are relatively clear. |
| Adaptive | Useful when the problem is understood but the best solution is uncertain, or when requirements will emerge through experimentation and feedback. |
| Hybrid | Common in larger organisations where governance, funding, and reporting require formal planning while delivery teams still need iterative learning. |

Adaptive work does not mean the analyst stops analysing. It means analysis, prioritisation, validation, and stakeholder learning happen repeatedly as the solution evolves.

### BA Competencies in Practice

The lecture connects BABOK knowledge areas with underlying competencies. Knowledge areas describe the work; competencies describe the personal and professional capabilities that make the work effective.

Important competencies include:

- Analytical thinking and problem solving.
- Communication and active listening.
- Facilitation and collaboration.
- Stakeholder awareness.
- Business knowledge and domain learning.
- Technology awareness.
- Adaptability in uncertain environments.
- Judgement about value, risk, and feasibility.

These competencies are transferable because they help analysts create clarity in complex situations, even when tools, titles, methods, or organisational structures change.

### Frameworks as Mental Scaffolding

BA frameworks and techniques do not always appear explicitly in workplace conversations. An analyst may not show every framework to stakeholders, but the framework still guides the questions asked, the information gathered, and the way findings are organised.

For example:

- BACCO helps the analyst check whether change, need, solution, stakeholder, value, and context have all been considered.
- Stakeholder analysis helps identify whose knowledge, authority, or support is required.
- Problem analysis helps prevent premature commitment to a solution.
- Requirements lifecycle management helps keep requirements aligned as the initiative changes.

The practical value of a framework is that it improves judgement and consistency, not that it must always be presented formally.

### Business Value and Fit for Purpose

A recurring theme is that business analysis should remain focused on value. A solution may be technically feasible but still poor if it does not address the need, fit the organisational context, or create useful outcomes for stakeholders.

The analyst therefore helps test:

- Whether the problem or opportunity is worth addressing.
- Whether stakeholders agree on the desired outcome.
- Whether options are feasible and valuable.
- Whether requirements trace back to the need.
- Whether the delivered solution is fit for purpose.

- Milani's use of BACCO aligns with the practical point that analysts need a structured way to connect needs, stakeholders, solutions, value, context, and change.
- Milani's process view supports the lecture's end-to-end framing: analysts may contribute from early problem framing through delivery support and solution evaluation.
- The lecture's emphasis on transferable BA competencies aligns with Milani's discussion of analytical thinking, communication, facilitation, business knowledge, and technology understanding.

## Summary

- Business analysis is a professional capability that can be applied across many roles and career stages.
- Requirements are elicited and analysed; they are not usually handed to the analyst in a complete form.
- Practical BA work spans ideation, problem analysis, option assessment, planning, requirements, delivery support, and solution validation.
- Predictive, adaptive, and hybrid approaches are chosen based on uncertainty, complexity, governance, and the need for learning.
- Frameworks and techniques support practical judgement by giving analysts structured ways to ask questions and organise information.
- Effective digital business analysis creates clarity, aligns stakeholders, and keeps technology-enabled change connected to business value.
