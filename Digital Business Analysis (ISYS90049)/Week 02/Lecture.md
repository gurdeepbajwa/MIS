## Topic

Business Analysis Planning and Monitoring

## Key Concepts

### BA Planning and Monitoring Tasks

| Task | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Plan BA Approach | Lists the main activities, tasks, methods, tools, and deliverables. The need or problem being investigated and relevant policies are also taken into account. |
| Plan Stakeholder Engagement | Identifies relevant stakeholders and plans how interaction, collaboration, and stakeholder management will be conducted. |
| Plan BA Governance | Clarifies who makes project-related decisions and how those decisions are made. |
| Plan BA Information Management | Defines how information generated during the project will be documented, stored, made available during the project, and retained for future use. |
| Identify BA Performance Improvements | Uses performance metrics to assess whether business analysis work can be improved systematically. |

### Planning Aspects

| Aspect | Guiding question |
| --- | --- |
| Background | What is the perceived need, problem, or opportunity to be investigated? What is the objective of the analysis work? |
| Context | What internal and external aspects affect the business and the analysis work? |
| Scope | What is the scope of the project? |
| Approach | What main approach will be taken, such as predictive or adaptive? |
| Activities | How will the outcome be achieved, including activities, planning, timing, methods, and tools? |
| Complexity and Risk | What level of complexity does the project have? What risks exist, and how will identified complexities and risks be handled? |
| Approval | How will approval be gained, and how will the resources required to do the work be secured? |

Milani links these aspects to the business analysis plan. The plan does not need to invent a new method for every initiative; if the organisation already has standards, templates, decision points, or development policies, the analyst adapts the approach to the specific change initiative.

The business analysis plan is a living document. It may be approved at the start of the work, but it should be updated when scope, approach, governance, stakeholder access, or information needs change.

### Predictive and Adaptive Approaches

| Aspect | Predictive approach | Adaptive approach |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Solution definition | Defined before implementation to maximise control and minimise risk. | Defined in iterations to arrive at the best solution or improve an existing solution. |
| Level of formality | Formal information is captured in standardised templates. | Informal information is gathered through team interaction and feedback. |
| Activities | Activities required to complete deliverables are identified first and then divided into tasks. | Activities are divided into iterations with deliverables first, and then the associated tasks are identified. |
| Timing | Tasks are performed in specific phases. | Tasks are performed iteratively. |

The choice does not need to be purely predictive or purely adaptive. Milani notes that organisations often use a hybrid approach, with some parts of the analysis planned formally and other parts handled iteratively.

Predictive approaches fit better when the problem, desired outcome, solution direction, and timeframe are relatively clear. Adaptive approaches fit better when there is uncertainty about the solution or when learning is expected through iteration. Large organisations often use hybrid approaches because governance and funding may require predictive planning while delivery teams still need adaptive discovery.

The BA approach is related to, but not the same as, project management. A project manager coordinates delivery work, resourcing, milestones, and project cadence. A business analyst clarifies the need, translates stakeholder concerns into requirements and analysis outputs, and checks that the proposed solution remains aligned with business value.

### Tasks and Techniques

A business analysis task is a unit of work the analyst needs to perform, such as planning stakeholder engagement or assessing requirement changes. A technique is a method used to perform the work, such as interviews, workshops, document analysis, or prioritisation methods.

### Complexity and Risk in BA Planning

When planning business analysis work, complexity and risk focus on the business analysis work itself, not only the eventual solution.

| Area | Factors to consider |
| --- | --- |
| Project complexity | Size of the organisation; number of business units; number of systems affected; number of stakeholders and their geographical distribution; size of the change initiative. |
| Project risk | Experience of the business analyst; domain knowledge of the analyst; stakeholder experience with change initiatives; stakeholder attitudes; stakeholder availability; time or financial restrictions. |

Risk treatment should identify how the analysis work will respond to uncertainty. Treatments may include adding experienced analysts, changing the elicitation method, adjusting scope, securing stakeholder access earlier, increasing review points, or allowing more time for complex analysis.

### Business Analysis Governance

A governance approach clarifies how business analysis work and change-related decisions will be controlled.

Key governance questions:

- How will business analysis work be approached and prioritised?
- What process will be used to propose changes to business analysis information?
- Who has authority and responsibility to propose changes?
- Who should be involved in change discussions?
- Who is responsible for analysing change requests?
- Who has authority to approve changes?
- How will changes be documented and communicated?

Governance elements:

| Element | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Decision Making Process | Defines how decisions about business analysis work are made. |
| Change Control Process | Defines how proposed changes are raised, assessed, approved, and managed. |
| Plan for Approvals | Clarifies what needs approval, who approves it, and when approval is required. |
| Plan Prioritisation Approach | Defines how business analysis work, changes, or requirements will be prioritised. |

Additional governance details:

- Decision making should specify how decisions will be made, what happens if stakeholders cannot agree, escalation paths, and who has final authority.
- Change control should define how changes are requested and should capture cost and time estimates, expected benefits, risks, priority, and possible courses of action.
- Prioritisation may be influenced by timelines, expected value, dependencies, resource constraints, and the adopted methodology.
- The prioritisation approach should define the level of formality, participants, techniques, decision criteria, and stakeholder roles.
- Approvals should confirm that BA outputs are sufficiently detailed, accurate, adequate, and useful for decision makers. Too much approval can slow the process and become bureaucratic.
- Information management should define how project information is documented, stored, shared during the project, and retained for future use. It should also consider privacy, access rights, storage location, reliance on third-party tools, and how sensitive stakeholder or organisational information will be protected.

Milani emphasises that the business analyst has an advisory role. The analyst investigates, analyses, and recommends, but business decisions about resources, scope, requirements, priorities, and approvals must be made by authorised stakeholders.

### BA Performance Improvements

Performance improvement means assessing and improving the business analysis work itself. It is separate from evaluating whether the final solution is effective.

Possible areas for BA performance measures:

| Measure area | What it assesses |
| --- | --- |
| Time | Whether BA tasks, deliverables, milestones, and reviews are completed within planned timeframes. |
| Accuracy and completeness | Whether analysis outputs are correct, relevant, and accepted without excessive rework. |
| Relevance or value | Whether produced information is useful for later decisions and tasks, rather than becoming unused documentation. |
| Strategic fit | Whether the analysis work identifies needs and recommendations that support business objectives. |
| Knowledge and skills | Whether the analyst has the domain knowledge, methods, and experience needed for the initiative. |
| Organisational support | Whether the analyst can access required stakeholders, resources, documents, and decision makers. |

Measures should be chosen carefully because they influence behaviour. For example, measuring only speed may encourage fast but shallow analysis.

## Summary

- Business analysis planning and monitoring defines how BA work will be carried out, governed, documented, and improved.
- Good planning makes the BA process more transparent by clarifying activities, stakeholders, decisions, information management, planning approach, and performance expectations.
- Planning should consider the problem or need being investigated, relevant policies, project context, project scope, approach, complexity, risk, approval, and future reuse of project information.
- Complexity and risk assessment should consider the conditions affecting the BA work, such as organisational scale, systems affected, stakeholder distribution, analyst capability, stakeholder attitudes, and time or budget limits.
- The outputs of BA planning and monitoring guide later analysis tasks, and once the business analysis plan is approved the analysis work can begin.
- The textbook extends the lecture by showing that governance and planning are mechanisms for aligning analysis work with organisational decision rights, policies, resources, and improvement practices.
