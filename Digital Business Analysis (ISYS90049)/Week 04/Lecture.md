## Topic

Elicitation and Collaboration

## Key Concepts

### Scope Definition

Scope definition clarifies the boundaries of the change being investigated. It helps stakeholders agree on:

- The extent of the change.
- Which components are relevant.
- Where business analysis effort should be applied.
- What is outside the scope.
- How to reduce the risk of scope creep.

Types of scope:

| Scope type | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| Solution scope | What changes are required to solve the problem or satisfy the need. |
| Product scope | Features, functions, systems, external entities, and resources relevant to the product. |
| Project scope | Work that needs to be performed to deliver the product or service. |

In planning, the analyst should distinguish between the scope of business analysis work and the later scope of the solution. BA work scope defines what analysis activities will be performed; solution scope defines what aspects of the product, system, process, or organisation may change.

### POPIT Model

POPIT is a holistic model for considering organisational change:

| Element | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| People | Stakeholders, employees, skills, experience, culture, and capability. |
| Organisation | Structure, business model, roles, and responsibilities. |
| Process | Business processes used to create value. |
| Information and Technology | Information, data, hardware, software, and supporting systems. |

POPIT is useful for defining project scope because it prevents the analyst from treating change as only a technology issue.

### Stakeholder Engagement

A stakeholder is someone who has an interest in the change, is affected by it, or shares the problem or need.

Stakeholder engagement matters because business analysis depends on productive communication, collaboration, support, and participation. Strong engagement can:

- Improve communication and reduce resistance.
- Clarify expectations and reduce scope creep.
- Increase stakeholder satisfaction.
- Reduce conflict during analysis.
- Improve the quality of elicited information.

### Stakeholder Engagement Process

| Step | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Identify stakeholders | Find relevant internal and external stakeholders. |
| Analyse stakeholders | Understand their involvement, attitudes, influence, impact, and needs. |
| Manage stakeholders | Plan communication and collaboration strategies for each stakeholder group. |

### Stakeholder Identification

Common stakeholder types include:

- Customer.
- Domain expert.
- End user.
- Implementation expert.
- Operational support.
- Project manager.
- Product owner.
- Regulator.
- Sponsor.
- Supplier.
- Tester.

Customers should not be assumed to be stakeholders in the same way in every initiative. The analyst should consider whether they are direct or indirect stakeholders, whether only certain customer segments are relevant, and whether customer input is actually needed for the problem being analysed.

### Stakeholder Analysis

Stakeholder analysis helps the analyst decide how to work with different stakeholders.

#### Level of Involvement

The stakeholder onion diagram analyses how close stakeholders are to the core of the initiative. Stakeholders nearer the centre are more directly involved in analysis and solution development; stakeholders further out may be affected or consulted but less involved day to day.

#### Attitude

Stakeholders may be categorised by attitude:

- Active support.
- Passive support.
- Neutral.
- Passive opposition.
- Active opposition.

Understanding attitude helps the analyst plan how to build buy-in and collaboration.

#### Influence and Impact

The stakeholder matrix maps stakeholders by influence and impact:

| Category | Influence | Impact | Management approach |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Key Players | High | High | Work closely and maintain agreement. |
| Keep Satisfied | High | Low | Ensure they remain satisfied. |
| Keep Informed | Low | High | Maintain a strong flow of information. |
| Monitor | Low | Low | Watch for changes in interest or influence. |

Stakeholder analysis should not treat engagement, support, and decision authority as the same thing. A stakeholder may be cooperative and available but have limited authority, while a decision maker may be supportive but hard to access. The analyst needs to understand both who can contribute useful knowledge and who can approve or block change.

Stakeholder attitudes can also be shaped by incentives. Someone may be useful to the analysis but still resist a proposed change if the change threatens their workload, status, autonomy, or perceived value.

### Stakeholder Management Plan

A stakeholder management plan defines:

- Why each stakeholder is involved.
- What information they need.
- When communication or collaboration should occur.
- How communication should occur.
- Where communication or collaboration will happen.

The plan may be developed through brainstorming, workshops, stakeholder interviews, discussions with other analysts, and lessons from similar completed projects.

### Elicitation

Elicitation means drawing out information. Business analysts elicit facts, perspectives, requirements, assumptions, constraints, and design-relevant information throughout the business analysis process.

Elicitation can be:

- Planned: the analyst prepares who to speak with, what to ask, what method to use, and what outcome is needed.
- Unplanned: useful information emerges through informal conversations or interactions.

### Elicitation Process

| Step | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Prepare elicitation | Understand the desired outcome and scope, choose techniques, prepare stakeholders, set up logistics, and prepare supporting materials. |
| Conduct elicitation | Gather information through collaboration, research, or experiments. |
| Confirm results | Check accuracy and consistency against sources and other elicited information. |
| Communicate results | Share requirements, designs, findings, alternatives, progress, or decisions with relevant stakeholders. |

### Types of Elicitation

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Collaborative | The analyst interacts with stakeholders to use their knowledge, experience, and perspectives. |
| Research | The analyst discovers, analyses, and synthesises information from documents, data, or other sources. |
| Experiment | The analyst uses controlled testing to discover information that is not already known. |

### Common Elicitation Techniques

| Technique | Use |
| --- | --- |
| Document analysis | Reviews existing documents, models, policies, instructions, reports, or data sources. |
| Interview | Elicits in-depth qualitative information from stakeholders. |
| Survey | Collects structured information from a larger group. |
| Observation | Studies work as it is performed. |
| Workshop | Brings stakeholders together to elicit, model, validate, or decide. |
| Focus group | Gathers feedback from a selected group with relevant experience. |

Document analysis should assess relevance, structure content for systematic review, compare and contrast sources, and identify gaps or conflicts.

Interviews can be structured, semi-structured, or unstructured. They are useful for depth, context, concerns, and stakeholder perspectives, but they can be time-consuming.

Surveys are useful for reaching larger groups and supporting quantitative analysis, but they usually provide less depth and may suffer from low response rates.

### Textbook Connections

- Milani Chapter 7 connects to stakeholder engagement through stakeholder identification, stakeholder wheel, stakeholder analysis, stakeholder matrix, onion diagram, and stakeholder management planning.
- Milani Chapter 5 connects to elicitation through preparing, conducting, confirming, and communicating elicitation results.
- Milani frames stakeholder engagement as a foundation for collaboration, not a one-off list of people.
- Milani also treats elicitation as continuous. It occurs across the business analysis process, not only at the start of requirements work.

## Summary

- Scope definition clarifies what the analysis will and will not cover.
- POPIT helps analysts consider people, organisation, process, information, and technology as connected elements of change.
- Stakeholder engagement includes identifying, analysing, and managing stakeholders.
- Stakeholder analysis should consider involvement, attitude, influence, impact, and communication needs.
- Elicitation draws out information needed for analysis and solution design.
- Elicitation should be prepared, conducted, confirmed, and communicated so that results are accurate, useful, and shared with the right stakeholders.
