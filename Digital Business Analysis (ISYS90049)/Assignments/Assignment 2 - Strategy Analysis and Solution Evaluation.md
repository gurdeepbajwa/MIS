## Case Summary

CollectaLab is a Melbourne-based collectibles and fandom lifestyle retailer with a head office in Melbourne and a distribution centre in Truganina. It has 48 head office staff, 160 warehouse and fulfilment staff, and annual turnover of about $118 million. The company sells through its online store and selected retailers across Australia, New Zealand, and Singapore.

The business depends heavily on limited-edition product launches, pre-order campaigns, emotionally invested repeat customers, and trust in product authenticity and delivery updates. Its current customer service operation has not scaled with business growth. Enquiries arrive through email, Instagram DMs, website chat, Facebook Messenger, WhatsApp, and TikTok comments, but there is no centralised customer service platform or unified customer record.

Current service work relies on spreadsheets, screenshots, inbox history, staff memory, and direct messages between teams. This creates duplicated work, inconsistent responses, missed messages, slow issue resolution, and weak coordination between customer service, marketing, warehouse, fulfilment, and product teams. These problems become worse during launches and pre-order campaigns.

Mia Chen, the Managing Director, wants CollectaLab to become more digitally mature, customer-centric, scalable, and investor-ready. She is interested in generative AI, but the solution must avoid robotic or low-effort communication because CollectaLab's Gen Z customers value authentic brand voice and trust.

## 1. Introduction

CollectaLab Pty Ltd is a Melbourne-based collectibles and fandom lifestyle retailer operating across Australia, New Zealand and Singapore. The organisation has grown through limited-edition product launches, pre-order campaigns and emotionally invested repeat customers who expect accurate, timely and authentic communication. However, CollectaLab's customer service capability has not scaled at the same pace as its commercial growth. Customer enquiries are handled across email, Instagram DMs, website chat, Facebook Messenger, WhatsApp and TikTok comments, with no central customer service platform or unified customer record. As a result, staff rely on spreadsheets, screenshots, inbox history, memory and informal messages between teams.

This creates a business problem rather than a narrow operational inconvenience. Fragmented service channels increase the likelihood of missed messages, duplicated work, inconsistent responses and delayed issue resolution. These issues become more visible during product launches and pre-order campaigns, when customers expect reliable updates about stock, fulfilment, shipping and product availability. The current approach also limits management visibility because senior leaders cannot easily monitor response times, unresolved cases, repeated enquiries or service bottlenecks.

The purpose of this report is to conduct a strategy analysis and solution evaluation for CollectaLab's customer service problem. The report analyses the current state, identifies business requirements, defines a future state, sets business goals and SMART objectives, develops a change strategy, assesses implementation risks and presents a recommended solution. The analysis uses POPIT to show how the problem affects organisation, process, information technology and people, and is guided by Milani's (2019) digital business analysis framework and relevant weekly lecture concepts, including current-state analysis, customer journey mapping, gap analysis, readiness assessment and KPI-based solution evaluation.

The recommended direction is a centralised cross-channel customer service platform with a unified customer record, order-status visibility, internal knowledge base, workflow controls and governed AI-assisted response support. The solution is intended to improve service speed and consistency while preserving human judgement for complex, emotional or brand-sensitive customer interactions.

Zendesk Suite fits this need because it provides ticketing, a unified agent workspace, messaging, knowledge base management, AI support and reporting dashboards within one service platform (Zendesk, n.d.-a; Zendesk, n.d.-b). It can support CollectaLab's required workflow by bringing enquiries from multiple digital channels into one queue, linking cases to customer history, supporting approved response content through a knowledge base, and giving management visibility over service activity. Order, stock and fulfilment visibility should be achieved by integrating Zendesk with CollectaLab's existing e-commerce, warehouse or order management system, using a native connector where available or an API-based integration where required. Zendesk's Shopify integration shows how order data can be surfaced inside support tickets, which is relevant if CollectaLab uses Shopify or a comparable e-commerce platform (Zendesk, n.d.-c).

The platform should include five main capabilities. First, cross-channel ticketing would address ID-1 by creating one workflow for receiving, assigning, prioritising and tracking enquiries. Second, a unified customer and order history would address ID-2 by allowing staff to see previous conversations, order issues and resolution history before replying. Third, order, stock, fulfilment and pre-order status visibility would address ID-3 by reducing manual checking with warehouse, fulfilment and product teams. Fourth, a knowledge base with approved templates and AI-assisted draft responses would address ID-4 by improving consistency for repetitive enquiries while keeping human review for sensitive or brand-critical cases. Fifth, management dashboards would address ID-5 by giving Mia Chen and senior management visibility over response time, resolution performance, backlog, repeated follow-ups and platform adoption.

The solution should also include process and people changes. Customer service staff would be trained to use the platform as the required workflow rather than relying on screenshots, spreadsheets, inbox history or memory. Marketing, warehouse, fulfilment and product teams would need clear ownership rules for updating stock, shipping and pre-order information before customer-facing updates are sent. AI should operate as decision support for staff, not as a fully autonomous customer-facing agent.

### 6.2 KPIs and Solution Evaluation

The two selected KPIs focus on response time and enquiry resolution because they directly measure whether the solution improves the core service issues identified in the current state. Other measures, such as customer satisfaction, staff workload, backlog size or platform adoption, could also be useful, but they are less direct as the primary evaluation measures for this section. The centralised platform will capture the required data automatically by recording when each enquiry is received, first answered, categorised, resolved, reopened or linked to repeat customer follow-ups.

| KPI | Why it matters | Data source | Timeframe |
|---|---|---|---|
| Average customer response time | Measures whether reliable status information and centralised workflow are improving service speed. | The platform records the timestamp when an enquiry is received and the timestamp when the first response is sent. The measure is calculated as total first-response time divided by number of enquiries. | Establish a baseline before deployment, then report monthly for the first six months. |
| Enquiry resolution rate | Measures whether enquiries are being closed successfully rather than only answered quickly. | The platform records ticket category, resolution status, close date, reopened tickets and repeated follow-ups linked to the same customer or order. The measure is calculated as resolved enquiries divided by total enquiries received in the reporting period. | Establish a baseline before deployment, then report monthly for the first six months. |

These KPIs align with Milani's (2019) view that KPIs should measure key areas of business performance and support evaluation of whether the solution delivers value. They also reflect Week 11's guidance that good metrics should be clear, relevant, economical, adequate, quantifiable and credible. Response time is clear and economical because it can be calculated automatically from platform timestamps. Enquiry resolution rate is relevant because it measures whether customer issues are actually being closed rather than only acknowledged. Both KPIs should be monitored monthly so CollectaLab can identify trends, compare actual performance against expected improvement, and investigate root causes if performance does not improve after one or two reporting intervals.
