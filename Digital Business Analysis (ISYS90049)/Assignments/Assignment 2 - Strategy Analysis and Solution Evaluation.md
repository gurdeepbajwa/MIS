## Case Summary

CollectaLab is a Melbourne-based collectibles and fandom lifestyle retailer with a head office in Melbourne and a distribution centre in Truganina. It has 48 head office staff, 160 warehouse and fulfilment staff, and annual turnover of about $118 million. The company sells through its online store and selected retailers across Australia, New Zealand, and Singapore.

The business depends heavily on limited-edition product launches, pre-order campaigns, emotionally invested repeat customers, and trust in product authenticity and delivery updates. Its current customer service operation has not scaled with business growth. Enquiries arrive through email, Instagram DMs, website chat, Facebook Messenger, WhatsApp, and TikTok comments, but there is no centralised customer service platform or unified customer record.

Current service work relies on spreadsheets, screenshots, inbox history, staff memory, and direct messages between teams. This creates duplicated work, inconsistent responses, missed messages, slow issue resolution, and weak coordination between customer service, marketing, warehouse, fulfilment, and product teams. These problems become worse during launches and pre-order campaigns.

Mia Chen, the Managing Director, wants CollectaLab to become more digitally mature, customer-centric, scalable, and investor-ready. She is interested in generative AI, but the solution must avoid robotic or low-effort communication because CollectaLab's Gen Z customers value authentic brand voice and trust.

## Core Problem Definition

CollectaLab's main problem is not lack of demand. The main problem is that its customer service capability is fragmented, manual, and reactive, while customer expectations and business scale require fast, accurate, and coordinated service.

The strongest A2 angle is:

> CollectaLab needs a unified customer service and knowledge-management solution with selective AI support, so staff can handle repetitive enquiries faster while preserving human control over sensitive or brand-critical interactions.

## Likely Proposed Solution

Working solution direction:

**An omnichannel customer service platform with a unified customer record, internal knowledge base, order-status integration, workflow automation, and AI-assisted response drafting or triage.**

This is stronger than recommending a standalone chatbot because the case problem is broader than response speed. The deeper issue is fragmented information and weak coordination. A chatbot alone could make the problem worse if it gives customers fast but inaccurate answers.

Possible solution components:

- Centralised ticketing across email, Instagram, website chat, Facebook Messenger, WhatsApp, and TikTok.
- Unified customer and order history for customer service staff.
- Internal knowledge base for shipping, returns, authenticity, restocks, pre-order delays, and damaged packaging.
- Integration with order, inventory, warehouse, and fulfilment information.
- AI triage to classify enquiries by topic, urgency, customer sentiment, and channel.
- AI-assisted draft replies for repetitive questions, with human review before sending.
- Escalation rules for high-risk cases such as angry customers, damaged limited-edition items, retail partner complaints, legal or refund disputes, and public social media issues.
- Shared update workflow so marketing cannot publish "shipping now" claims unless fulfilment status is confirmed.

### 6.1 Proposed Solution

The recommended solution is a centralised omnichannel customer service platform with controlled AI support. This is more appropriate than a standalone chatbot because CollectaLab's main problem is not only response speed. The deeper issue is fragmented service information across channels, teams and customer records. The platform should capture enquiries from email, Instagram DMs, website chat, Facebook Messenger, WhatsApp and TikTok in one ticketing workflow, with each enquiry assigned an owner, status, priority, channel, customer record and order reference.

The platform should include five main capabilities. First, omnichannel ticketing would address ID-1 by creating one workflow for receiving, assigning, prioritising and tracking enquiries. Second, a unified customer and order history would address ID-2 by allowing staff to see previous conversations, order issues and resolution history before replying. Third, order, stock, fulfilment and pre-order status visibility would address ID-3 by reducing manual checking with warehouse, fulfilment and product teams. Fourth, a knowledge base with approved templates and AI-assisted draft responses would address ID-4 by improving consistency for repetitive enquiries while keeping human review for sensitive or brand-critical cases. Fifth, management dashboards would address ID-5 by giving Mia Chen and senior management visibility over response time, resolution performance, backlog, repeated follow-ups and platform adoption.

The solution should also include process and people changes. Customer service staff would be trained to use the platform as the required workflow rather than relying on screenshots, spreadsheets, inbox history or memory. Marketing, warehouse, fulfilment and product teams would need clear ownership rules for updating stock, shipping and pre-order information before customer-facing updates are sent. AI should be used as staff support, not as a fully autonomous customer-facing agent.

### 6.2 KPIs and Solution Evaluation

| KPI | Why it matters | Data source | Timeframe |
|---|---|---|---|
| Average customer response time | Measures whether reliable status information and centralised workflow are improving service speed. | The platform records the timestamp when an enquiry is received and the timestamp when the first response is sent. | Establish a baseline before deployment, then report monthly for the first six months. |
| Enquiry resolution rate | Measures whether enquiries are being closed successfully rather than only answered quickly. | The platform records ticket category, resolution status, close date, reopened tickets and repeated follow-ups linked to the same customer or order. | Establish a baseline before deployment, then report monthly for the first six months. |

These KPIs align with Milani's (2019) view that KPIs should measure key areas of business performance and support evaluation of whether the solution delivers value. They also reflect Week 11's guidance that good metrics should be clear, relevant, economical, adequate, quantifiable and credible. Response time is clear and economical because it can be calculated automatically from platform timestamps. Enquiry resolution rate is relevant because it measures whether customer issues are actually being closed rather than only acknowledged. Both KPIs should be monitored monthly so CollectaLab can identify trends, compare actual performance against expected improvement, and investigate root causes if performance does not improve after one or two reporting intervals.
