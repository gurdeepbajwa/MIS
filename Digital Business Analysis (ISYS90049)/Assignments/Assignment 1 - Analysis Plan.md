## 1. Background

The first problem is the fragmentation of customer communication channels. Customer inquiries come through emails, Instagram, and website chats while staff rely on informal methods such as spreadsheets, screenshots, or memory to track interactions. This shows a lack of a unified customer service system, causing inconsistent information and conflicting responses. Addressing this is essential because CollectaLab’s highly engaged customers expect fast and accurate communication, so inconsistent service can erode customer trust and damage brand reputation.

The second problem is cross-functional misalignment between departments. Marketing, fulfilment and customer service departments often operate with unsynchronized information, creating scenarios where products are promoted as “shipping now” despite unavailable stock. This demonstrates weak system integration and limited real-time data sharing, forcing customer service staff to manually verify information, creating bottlenecks, delays, and inaccurate responses. This is critical because it reduces operational efficiency, has already contributed to the loss of a major retail partnership and may increase staff frustration and burnout.

However, CollectaLab’s established customer base and digitally engaged market position creates opportunities for improvements through centralized systems and selective AI use to strengthen customer experience. Together, these changes could reduce inefficiencies, improve scalability, and help CollectaLab compete more effectively with AI-driven rivals.

## 2.3 PEST Analysis

**Political**

CollectaLab must comply with Australian consumer law regarding how it sells, markets, and supports products. Customers are entitled to remedies such as repairs, replacements, or refunds when goods fail to meet consumer guarantees (ACCC, 2024). For CollectaLab, inaccurate product information, misleading order updates, or unclear return policies could therefore translate directly into complaints, refund costs, and reputational damage. This matters because the case already points to trust and service issues, so weak communication controls could quickly become a legal and commercial problem rather than just an operational inconvenience.

The Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (2025) notes that commercial AI systems using personal information must meet privacy requirements. For CollectaLab, fragmented systems and future AI adoption increase the risk of improper storage, reuse, or disclosure of customer data. That could undermine customer trust and create compliance concerns at the same time the business is trying to modernise operations and present itself as a scalable business.

Geopolitical instability may also increase shipping, supply, and operating costs through higher oil prices, inflation, and weaker global growth (Oliver, 2026). For CollectaLab, this adds pressure to reduce internal inefficiencies because service errors, rework, and delays become more costly when the broader trading environment is already unstable.

**Economic**

CollectaLab faces pressure to reduce costs and improve efficiency because it is being prepared for a possible sale. In that context, fragmented customer service processes increase labour cost, reduce productivity, and weaken the business case for growth. The immediate significance is that poor service performance is not only a customer issue; it can also reduce perceived business value at a strategically sensitive time.

The company is also exposed to higher shipping and supply chain costs due to global instability such as the Iranian conflict. These increases may be passed on to consumers (Josephs, 2026), reducing demand. This means CollectaLab must manage both rising external cost pressure and internal inefficiencies. If service remains slow or inconsistent while costs rise, customers may become even less tolerant of delays or errors.

**Social**

CollectaLab’s customers are highly engaged online, emotionally attached to products, and quick to share positive or negative experiences on social media. They expect fast, transparent, and authentic communication (Sprout Social, 2018). In this market, poor service can quickly damage trust, reduce loyalty, and amplify dissatisfaction publicly. This intensifies the case need to improve because service quality directly affects customer sentiment and brand reputation in a visible digital environment.

**Technological**

Competitors are using AI-generated content, personalised recommendations, and 24/7 customer engagement, raising customer expectations for faster and more consistent support. CollectaLab still relies on fragmented platforms and manual responses, which limits service quality and efficiency. The gap matters because customer expectations are being shaped by technology-enabled rivals, not by CollectaLab’s current internal capability.

CRM systems, automation, and AI tools could help centralise customer interactions, improve response times, and support more consistent communication, but poor implementation may create inaccurate or overly robotic responses. This means technology is both an opportunity and a risk: the analysis must identify where digital tools would genuinely solve the business problem rather than simply add another disconnected layer.

## 5. Complexity and Risk

This section outlines the main complexities and risks in carrying out the analysis. The focus is on challenges faced by the analysts during the work, rather than on general business problems. Addressing these issues early is important because weak analysis quality would reduce the credibility and usefulness of the final recommendations.

## 5.1 Complexity

The first complexity is fragmented customer service information, which makes it difficult to build a complete and consistent evidence base. Differences in data formats and definitions across systems make direct comparison difficult and require additional effort to reconcile meanings across sources (Dutka et al., 2014; Putrama & Martinek, 2024). For example, teams may interpret labels such as "shipping now" differently, and customer or order information may be stored inconsistently across channels. To manage this complexity, the analysis should first identify a small set of critical data elements and then progressively align records into a common structure (Khoei & Singh, 2025; Ramadhani et al., 2016). This approach is suitable because it reduces noise, improves comparability, and makes inconsistencies easier to trace.

A second complexity is the presence of structural communication gaps between departments. Limited collaboration, combined with fragmented systems, reinforces knowledge silos and makes information harder to verify across the organisation (Romano & Albrecht, 2026). As a result, evidence collected from one department may be incomplete or biased. This can be managed by comparing multiple sources rather than relying on a single perspective and by using stakeholder mapping to clarify who owns which information. This is likely to work because cross-validation makes contradictions visible and reduces the chance that one departmental narrative shapes the whole analysis.

A third complexity is balancing competing priorities across stakeholders. Senior decision-makers may emphasise efficiency and sale readiness, while operational teams may prioritise workload relief and practicality. If these priorities are not surfaced clearly, the analysis may drift toward recommendations that are either strategically attractive but operationally unrealistic, or operationally sensible but not aligned with management priorities. This can be managed by documenting assumptions, using workshop activities to surface trade-offs explicitly, and evaluating future options against shared criteria such as feasibility, customer impact, and cost.
