## Topic

Strategy Analysis: Future State

## Key Concepts

### Future State Analysis

Future state analysis describes what the business should be like after the change has addressed the business need. It determines the necessary conditions that must exist for the business need to be met.

Future state analysis helps stakeholders understand:

- What success should look like.
- What value and impact should be realised.
- What outcomes the change is intended to deliver.
- Which parts of the organisation will need to change.
- What solution space should be considered before selecting a specific solution.

The future state should describe the desired "what", not the detailed "how". For example, it may define that a new customer relationship capability is required, but it should not prematurely decide that SAP CRM, Microsoft Dynamics, or a custom-built system is the final solution.

### Future State in Strategy Analysis

Strategy analysis connects the current state to the future state:

1. Analyse current state: What is the business like now?
2. Define future state: What should the business be like in the future?
3. Assess risks: What uncertainties could affect the ability to realise value?
4. Define change strategy: How should the organisation move from current state to future state?

The current state and future state descriptions should be structured in a compatible way where possible. Using similar elements, such as POPIT, makes it easier to compare the two states and identify gaps.

### Business Need, Requirement, Goals, and Objectives

Future state analysis decomposes the business need into more concrete levels.

| Level | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Business need | The high-level problem or opportunity that explains why change is required. |
| Business requirement | A high-level statement of what is required to address the need. |
| Business goal | A qualitative statement of what the business wants to achieve. |
| Business objective | A more concrete, measurable, and actionable target that supports a goal. |

The hierarchy moves from abstract to concrete:

Business need -> business requirement -> business goals -> SMART objectives.

In the lecture, goals and objectives are defined at the POPIT level:

| POPIT area | Future state focus |
| --- | --- |
| Organisation | What organisational structure, roles, teams, or responsibilities need to exist? |
| Processes | What processes need to be improved, digitised, automated, or redesigned? |
| Information and Technology | What systems, data, integration, infrastructure, or digital capability needs to exist? |
| People | What skills, training, capacity, or cultural changes are required? |

### SMART Objectives

Business goals are qualitative and longer-term. Business objectives make those goals more tangible and actionable.

SMART objectives should be:

| SMART element | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Specific | The objective describes a specific outcome. |
| Measurable | The outcome can be measured, and the required data is accessible. |
| Achievable | The outcome can be achieved with reasonable effort. |
| Relevant | The objective is aligned with the organisation's vision, mission, goals, and business need. |
| Time-bounded | The outcome is to be achieved within a defined time frame. |

Objectives should support the relevant goal, and goals should support the business requirement and business need.

### Solution Scope

The scope of the solution space describes which parts of the business need to change. It identifies what is in scope and out of scope for the change.

Solution scope is not the same as the final solution:

| Concept | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| Solution scope | What needs to change to achieve the future state. |
| Solution | How those changes will be made. |

For example, the solution scope might state that the purchase order process must be redesigned to use an enterprise purchasing module. Alternative solutions might then include different process redesigns, software packages, or implementation approaches.

The POPIT model can be used to structure solution scope:

- Organisation: roles, responsibilities, structures, decision paths.
- Processes: workflows, activities, rules, handoffs.
- Information and Technology: systems, data, integrations, digital tools.
- People: skills, training, capacity, acceptance, change readiness.

### Constraints

Constraints limit which future states, solution scopes, and change strategies are feasible.

Common constraints include:

- Budgetary limits.
- Time limits and deadlines.
- Technology constraints.
- Resource limits for implementation or future maintenance.
- Rules and regulations.
- Company policies.

Constraints should be made explicit because they shape solution options and trade-offs.

### Gap Analysis

Gap analysis compares the current state with the future state and asks what needs to be put in place to move from one to the other.

Gap analysis identifies:

- The difference between current and future states.
- Examples that illustrate the gap.
- The business unit responsible for the gap.
- Actions that could bridge the gap.
- The estimated size of effort required.

Typical gap analysis fields:

| Field | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Current state | What exists now. |
| Future state | What should exist after change. |
| Gap description | The difference between current and future state. |
| Example | A concrete illustration of the gap. |
| Responsible unit | The area that owns or is responsible for addressing the gap. |
| Actions | Possible ways to fill the gap. |
| Size estimate | Rough effort estimate, such as small, medium, or large. |

Gaps do not always stay within one POPIT category. For example, an information technology gap may require organisational policy changes and people awareness activities to resolve it.

### Change Strategy

A change strategy is a high-level project plan that specifies the key changes, related activities, and the approach for implementing the change.

It should explain:

- What key changes are required.
- Whether implementation should happen all at once or in stages.
- Why the chosen approach is efficient and effective.
- Whether the organisation is ready to make and sustain the change.
- How transition states and releases will work if implementation is staged.

### Implementation Approaches

| Approach | Meaning | Typical use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Big bang | All required changes are implemented in one project or one major release. | More suitable when the project is small or value cannot be realised until all parts are in place. |
| Gradual implementation | A large change is broken into smaller projects or stages. | Useful when the project is large, complex, risky, or disruptive. |
| Incremental implementation | The solution is delivered in increments that progressively add capability or coverage. | Useful when value can be realised in parts and learning can be incorporated. |
| Iterative implementation | Work cycles revisit and refine the solution based on feedback and learning. | Useful when uncertainty is high and the best path needs repeated adjustment. |

The implementation approach should fit the size, risk, complexity, stakeholder readiness, and value-realisation pattern of the change.

### Enterprise Readiness and Transition Planning

Enterprise readiness assessment asks whether the organisation is ready to make the change, sustain the solution, and realise the intended value.

Readiness issues may include:

- Stakeholder support or resistance.
- User acceptance of the new solution.
- Training needs.
- Availability of change champions.
- Organisational capacity to maintain the solution.
- Communication and feedback channels.

Transition planning defines how the organisation moves through stages toward the future state. If the change is staged, release planning should clarify what will be released, when, and how each stage contributes to the future state.

### Risk Assessment

A risk is an uncertain event that might affect the project, objectives, or ability to realise the future state.

Risk analysis should consider:

- Consequences and impact.
- Probability or likelihood.
- Timing: when the risk might occur.
- Signs that indicate the risk may be approaching.
- Risk tolerance.

Risk treatment options include:

| Treatment | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Avoid | Prevent the risk from occurring. |
| Transfer | Transfer or share the risk with another party. |
| Mitigate | Reduce the probability of the risk or reduce its impact. |
| Accept | Take no preventive action and manage the risk if it occurs. |
| Increase readiness | Identify warning signs and prepare to respond faster if the risk emerges. |

Risks can be documented in a risk register.

## Textbook Connections

Milani Chapter 13 connects directly to future state analysis. Milani explains that the future state determines the necessary conditions for addressing business needs, while avoiding premature commitment to a detailed solution.

### Future State Description

Milani states that future state descriptions may include organisational structures, skills, facilities, business processes, data and information flows, information systems, interfaces, architecture, and technology infrastructure.

The future state can be described using text, diagrams, models, statistics, or a combination. It does not need to perfectly mirror the current state, but overlap is useful because it supports completeness and makes gap analysis easier.

### Level of Detail

Milani emphasises the balance between being too vague and too detailed:

- If the future state is too vague, stakeholders may not understand what success means.
- If it is too detailed, the analyst may prematurely restrict creative solution options.
- Key or high-value areas may deserve more detailed analysis because missing them creates greater risk.

The future state should be detailed enough to define outcomes, value, solution scope, constraints, assumptions, organisational impact, and stakeholder agreement.

### Goals and Objectives

Milani explains that goals and objectives add specificity to business needs and business requirements. Goals are qualitative statements about what the organisation wants to achieve. Objectives decompose those goals into measurable targets.

The lecture's SMART objectives align with Milani's emphasis that objectives should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bounded. Milani also notes that the terminology can be blurry in practice; the important point is to understand why the initiative exists, what effect is expected, and how success can be assessed.

### Digital Future State

Milani argues that when working with digital technologies, the future state should explain how digital technologies may enhance the solution, deliver value, and enable further development. Digital technologies should not only fix a current problem; they may also create future value through new functionality, automation, data use, or improved customer experience.

### Change Strategy

Milani Chapter 14 connects to the lecture's discussion of gap analysis, change strategy, implementation approach, readiness, and transition planning. Milani describes change strategy as the work of determining how the future state can become the new current state.

Important textbook links:

- Gap analysis identifies what must be put in place for the organisation to meet its needs.
- Solution scope becomes clearer as understanding improves.
- Alternative solutions should be compared before the final solution is selected.
- Readiness matters because a strong solution on paper may fail if the organisation cannot adopt or sustain it.
- Delivery may happen in one release or in increments depending on cost, time, quality, risk, and stakeholder readiness.

## Summary

- Future state analysis describes what the business should look like after change.
- It clarifies success, value, outcomes, and the solution space before selecting a specific solution.
- Business needs are decomposed into business requirements, goals, and SMART objectives.
- Solution scope defines what needs to change; the solution defines how those changes will be made.
- Gap analysis compares current and future states to identify required changes.
- Change strategy defines the high-level implementation approach, readiness work, and transition path.
- Risk analysis identifies uncertainties that may affect the transition from current state to future state.
