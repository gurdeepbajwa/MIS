## Topic

Context Analysis

## Key Concepts

### Business Context

Business analysis does not examine a need in isolation. A problem, need, or opportunity exists inside several layers of context:

| Context layer | What it means for analysis |
| --- | --- |
| Immediate context | The local situation around the problem, need, or opportunity being analysed. |
| Organisational context | The organisation's strategy, business model, capabilities, stakeholders, structure, culture, and policies. |
| Industry context | Competitive forces, industry structure, suppliers, customers, substitutes, and new entrants. |
| External environment | Broader political, economic, social, technological, legal, and environmental influences. |

Context analysis helps the business analyst understand what influences the problem and what may constrain or enable possible solutions.

### External Context Analysis

External context analysis considers influences outside the organisation. The lecture focuses on PEST analysis and Porter's Five Forces.

#### PEST Analysis

PEST is a structured way to analyse broad external influences:

| Factor | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| Political | Government regulation, political stability, safety laws, data protection, tariffs, and public policy. |
| Economic | Interest rates, inflation, market growth, economic conditions, exchange rates, and cost pressures. |
| Social | Population growth, age distribution, lifestyle, attitudes, culture, education levels, and customer expectations. |
| Technological | Technological advances, availability of technology, IT infrastructure, and digital capability. |

PESTLE extends PEST by also considering legal and environmental influences.

When using PEST, focus on:

- What is relevant to the organisation or the specific problem area.
- What is likely to happen, not every possible external event.
- What trends are emerging over the next few years.
- How the factors could affect the organisation, problem, or solution.
- What evidence or data supports the analysis.

#### Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces analyses industry attractiveness and competitive pressure:

| Force | Core question |
| --- | --- |
| Rivalry among existing competitors | How intense is competition between current industry players? |
| Threat of new entrants | How easy is it for new competitors to enter the industry? |
| Threat of substitutes | What alternative products or services could meet the same need? |
| Bargaining power of customers | How much power do buyers have over price, quality, or terms? |
| Bargaining power of suppliers | How much power do suppliers have over cost, quality, or availability? |

This framework is useful when business analysis is strategic or externally focused, such as product development, market positioning, or digital service enhancement.

### Internal Business Context

Internal context covers factors within the organisation but outside the immediate scope of the problem being analysed.

Important internal aspects include:

- Organisational strategy.
- Business model and capabilities.
- Stakeholders.
- Organisational structure and culture.
- Internal policies, standards, and templates.

### Organisational Strategy

Organisational strategy explains the long-term direction and scope of the organisation. It shapes business analysis because a suitable solution should support what the organisation is trying to achieve.

Key components:

| Component | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Objectives | What is the organisation trying to achieve? |
| Plans and policies | How does the organisation intend to achieve its objectives? |
| Markets or business areas | Where does the organisation compete or operate? |
| Stakeholder value | What financial and non-financial value does the organisation aim to create? |

Porter's generic strategies can also help position an organisation by scope and source of advantage:

- Cost leadership: broad market, low cost.
- Differentiation: broad market, distinctive value.
- Cost focus: narrow market, low cost.
- Differentiation focus: narrow market, specialised value.

### Business Model and Capabilities

The business model describes how the organisation creates, delivers, and captures value. Milani connects this to tools such as the Business Model Canvas, which considers elements such as customer segments, value proposition, channels, customer relationships, key activities, key resources, key partners, cost structure, and revenue streams.

Business capabilities describe what the organisation can do. In digital business analysis, capabilities matter because a digital solution only creates value if the organisation has, or can develop, the capabilities needed to sustain it.

### SWOT Analysis

SWOT combines internal and external context:

| Dimension | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Strengths | Internal factors that help the organisation. |
| Weaknesses | Internal factors that harm or limit the organisation. |
| Opportunities | External factors that could be exploited. |
| Threats | External factors that could create risk or pressure. |

SWOT is useful for:

- Identifying needs when they are not yet clearly known.
- Understanding the context of a change initiative.
- Identifying what to exploit, protect, or improve.
- Comparing possible options for meeting organisational needs.
- Anticipating barriers to success and planning responses.

### Textbook Connections

- Milani Chapter 2 explains external context through PEST, Porter's Five Forces, and industry trends.
- Milani Chapter 3 explains internal context through organisational strategy, business model analysis, business capabilities, stakeholders, culture, structure, internal policies, and SWOT.
- The lecture follows Milani's idea that context analysis should be purpose-driven. The analyst should not analyse every possible factor, only those relevant to the problem, opportunity, or strategic decision.
- Milani emphasises evidence. PEST and SWOT may involve interpretation, but the analysis should be grounded in data where possible.

## Summary

- Context analysis helps business analysts understand the broader conditions around a problem, need, or opportunity.
- PEST examines broad external forces, while Porter's Five Forces examines industry-level competitive forces.
- Internal context analysis considers strategy, business model, capabilities, stakeholders, structure, culture, and policies.
- SWOT brings internal and external analysis together by identifying strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats.
- Strong context analysis helps avoid solutions that look technically attractive but do not fit the organisation, industry, or external environment.
