## Purpose

Use the Five-Why method to move from a visible symptom toward a deeper cause that can inform requirements, solution options, or corrective actions.

## When to Use It

Use Five Whys when:

- the problem is narrow enough to trace through a causal chain;
- stakeholders can explain how work actually happens;
- the analyst needs to challenge a first explanation before recommending a solution;
- there is enough evidence to test each answer.

Do not use it by itself when the issue is complex, political, cross-functional, or likely to have several interacting causes. In those cases, combine it with interviews, workshops, fishbone analysis, operational data, or Pareto analysis.

## Steps

1. State the problem precisely.
2. Ask why the problem occurs.
3. Treat the answer as a possible cause, not as proven truth.
4. Ask why that cause occurs.
5. Continue until the answer identifies a deeper cause that can be acted on.
6. Check each step against evidence.
7. Stop when further questioning no longer improves the analysis or when the next answer requires a different investigation method.
8. Translate the finding into implications for requirements, solution options, controls, or further analysis.

## Evidence Checks

For each "why" answer, ask:

- Who knows this directly?
- What data supports it?
- Is this a cause, a symptom, or an assumption?
- Could there be another causal path?
- Does this explanation apply across cases or only to one example?

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Better Practice |
| --- | --- |
| Stopping at the first plausible answer | Continue until the cause explains recurrence or prevention. |
| Treating stakeholder opinion as fact | Validate with data, observation, documents, or other stakeholders. |
| Forcing one linear cause | Use a fishbone diagram or workshop when there are multiple cause categories. |
| Asking "why" in a blaming tone | Use neutral prompts such as "what causes that?" or "what makes that happen?" |
| Jumping from cause to solution | First check whether the cause is material, recurrent, and worth addressing. |

## Exam Use

In an exam answer, Five Whys is strongest when it shows that the analyst is avoiding premature solution selection. A good answer should mention:

- symptom versus cause;
- stakeholder evidence;
- validation with data;
- limitations of one causal chain;
- connection to later requirements or solution options.

## Retrieval Prompts

1. When is Five Whys useful in problem analysis?
2. Why should Five-Why answers be treated as hypotheses rather than facts?
3. What are two limitations of the Five-Why method?
4. How can Five Whys support better requirements?
