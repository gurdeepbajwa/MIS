## Topic

Problem Analysis

## Key Concepts

### Business Needs and Problem Analysis

Business analysis begins with needs. A business need explains why change may be required, usually because of a problem or opportunity of strategic importance.

Problem analysis goes deeper than naming the need. It systematically investigates why the problem exists and what causes it.

| Concept | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Business need | The problem or opportunity that explains why something may need to change. |
| Problem analysis | The process of identifying and evaluating the reasons behind a problem or set of issues. |
| Root cause analysis | A systematic examination of the origin of a problem, rather than only its visible effects. |
| Symptom | An observable effect of the problem. |
| Cause | A reason the problem exists. |
| Root cause | A deeper cause that, if removed or addressed, helps prevent recurrence. |

### Why Problem Analysis Matters

Without a solid understanding of the problem and its causes, an organisation may choose a solution that looks reasonable but does not solve the real issue.

Problem analysis helps the analyst:

- Separate causes from symptoms.
- Understand how stakeholders perceive the problem.
- Identify what evidence supports each explanation.
- Avoid jumping to a solution too early.
- Focus effort on the causes that matter most.

### Reactive and Preventive Problem Analysis

| Approach | Focus | Orientation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reactive | Current business problems already affecting stakeholders. | Tactical, focused on existing root causes. |
| Preventive | Potential problem areas and future opportunities. | Strategic, forward-looking, and proactive. |

### Root Cause Analysis Process

Root cause analysis is usually iterative rather than linear.

Typical steps:

1. Define the problem.
2. Collect relevant data.
3. Identify possible causes.
4. Evaluate which causes are most important.
5. Identify corrective actions that prevent or reduce recurrence.
6. Revisit earlier steps when new evidence changes the understanding of the problem.

Problem analysis should combine qualitative and quantitative evidence. Facts should be supported by data where possible; unsupported views should be treated as opinions until validated.

Triangulation improves problem analysis. Interviews, workshops, and focus groups provide depth, but surveys, operational data, customer data, employee data, or system reports can help validate whether the perceived problem is supported by evidence.

### Problem Definition

Problem definition creates clarity about what is being solved and what a successful solution would need to address.

Key aspects of a problem definition:

| Aspect | Guiding questions |
| --- | --- |
| Background and context | What is the current situation? Why is it problematic? What led to this situation? |
| Problem | What is the problem more precisely? How is it affecting the business? |
| Stakeholders | Who is affected? How are they affected? |
| Potential challenges | What issues, challenges, or opportunities should be considered? |

### Problem Statement

A problem statement is a clear articulation of the problem to be solved. It should make the analysis focus explicit.

A useful problem statement often identifies:

- The problem.
- The affected stakeholder or stakeholder group.
- The impact on stakeholders, work, value, or business outcomes.

Common problem areas include:

- Strategy: ineffective positioning or inability to adapt.
- Products and services: quality, functionality, or innovation issues.
- Processes: manual work, inefficient workflows, or lack of standardisation.
- IT systems: outdated technology, weak integration, poor data, or high maintenance costs.
- Infrastructure: inflexible, insecure, or capacity-limited foundations.

### Cause Identification

Cause identification asks: why does the problem exist?

Root causes can be difficult to identify because:

- A problem may have multiple causes.
- Some causes contribute more strongly than others.
- Stakeholders may describe symptoms as if they are causes.
- The real cause may sit outside the knowledge of the people initially involved.

### Five-Why Method

The Five-Why method repeatedly asks why a problem occurs until a deeper cause is identified.

The analyst can also ask related questions:

- How come?
- What makes you think that?
- How does that work?
- What is the relationship?
- How often does it happen?
- Does it occur at specific times or randomly?
- Are there warning signals?
- Who is involved?

Limitations:

- There may be several valid causal paths.
- Answers need evidence, not only opinion.
- It is easy to stop at a symptom too early.
- The right stakeholders or domain experts need to be involved.
- Complex problems may require a workshop rather than a single chain of questioning.
- The conversation can drift toward an unrelated concern if the analyst does not keep the questioning anchored to the original problem.

### Fishbone Diagram

A fishbone diagram, also called an Ishikawa or cause-and-effect diagram, organises possible causes of a problem into categories.

It is useful when a problem may have multiple root causes.

The problem or effect is placed at the head of the diagram. Main branches represent primary cause categories, and smaller branches represent more specific contributing causes. The analyst can use established categories when they fit, or define categories from the situation being analysed.

Common 6M categories:

| Category | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| Machine | Technology, functionality, interface, or system integration issues. |
| Method | How work is designed, understood, communicated, trained, or performed. |
| Material | Input problems, including poor data, missing data, incorrect data, or physical materials. |
| Man | People-related causes such as skills, training, motivation, or workload. |
| Measurement | Estimation, calculation, reporting, or data-reading problems. |
| Milieu | Environmental, social, physical, contextual, or dependency-related causes. |

Fishbone diagrams can also be used for opportunities by treating the "problem" as a desired goal and the causes as enablers or requirements.

### Pareto Analysis

Pareto analysis applies the 80/20 principle: a small number of causes may account for a large share of the problem.

In problem analysis, Pareto analysis helps the analyst identify which causes deserve the most attention.

Pareto analysis does not discover root causes by itself. It is most useful after possible causes have already been identified through techniques such as Five-Why analysis, fishbone diagrams, document analysis, or stakeholder elicitation.

Typical use:

1. Gather data about causes or issue occurrences.
2. Sort causes by frequency or impact.
3. Calculate cumulative frequency or cumulative impact.
4. Visualise the result in a Pareto chart.
5. Focus first on the vital few causes with the greatest effect.

### Textbook Connections

- Milani emphasises that analysis should identify real causes, not only visible effects.

## Summary

- Problem analysis explains why a business need exists.
- Root cause analysis prevents the analyst from treating symptoms as the real problem.
- A clear problem definition includes context, problem, stakeholders, impacts, and potential challenges.
- The Five-Why method explores causal chains, fishbone diagrams organise multiple possible causes, and Pareto analysis helps prioritise the most significant causes.
- Strong problem analysis improves the chance that later requirements and solutions address the real business issue.
