## Section 1 - Planning

Questions 1 and 2 are based on the following case.

MetroFresh Grocers is an Australian grocery retailer with 38 stores across metropolitan and regional areas. The organisation has a strong local reputation but is losing customers to competitors with more reliable online ordering and faster in-store service. Customers complain that online stock levels are inaccurate, deliveries are delayed, and store staff cannot answer questions about online orders. Store managers report that inventory data is held across separate point-of-sale systems, warehouse spreadsheets, and an e-commerce platform. Staff often reconcile product availability manually at the end of the day. The executive team wants to improve customer retention, operational efficiency, and competitiveness. They have asked a Business Analyst to prepare a Business Analysis Plan within one month.

### Question 1 - 11 marks

Discuss THREE key challenges that the Business Analyst could encounter in preparing a good Business Analysis Plan for MetroFresh. (3 marks)

Discuss TWO key problems experienced by MetroFresh and TWO opportunities that MetroFresh can seize. In your discussion, explain why those problems and opportunities need to be addressed. (8 marks)

#### Question 1 Guide

Lecture hints:

- [[../Week 02/Lecture|Week 02 Lecture]] - BA planning, planning aspects, complexity and risk, governance, information management.
- [[../Week 03/Lecture|Week 03 Lecture]] - internal and external context, strategy, SWOT, PEST, Five Forces.
- [[../Week 04/Lecture|Week 04 Lecture]] - stakeholder identification, stakeholder analysis, elicitation.

How to answer:

- Separate BA planning challenges from MetroFresh's business problems. A planning challenge affects the analyst's ability to produce the plan.
- For each planning challenge, state the challenge, connect it to the case, and briefly explain why it threatens the quality of the BA plan.
- For problems, use concrete case facts such as customer retention, inaccurate stock data, delivery delays, disconnected systems, manual reconciliation, or staff workload.
- For opportunities, think about digital improvement and business value: integrated inventory, order visibility, customer communication, process standardisation, or improved online fulfilment.
- Do not simply list technology solutions. Explain why the issue matters to competitiveness, efficiency, customer experience, or decision quality.

### Question 2 - 14 marks

Discuss THREE techniques that would be useful for analysing the context of the MetroFresh problem. For each technique, explain how it would help the context analysis. (6 marks)

Propose FOUR key activities that the Business Analyst should plan to perform throughout the analysis work. For each activity, explain the importance of the activity and identify elicitation technique(s) likely to be useful. (8 marks)

#### Question 2 Guide

Lecture hints:

- [[../Week 03/Lecture|Week 03 Lecture]] - PEST, Porter's Five Forces, internal context, SWOT, strategy, business capabilities.
- [[../Week 04/Lecture|Week 04 Lecture]] - POPIT, stakeholder engagement, elicitation process, elicitation techniques.
- [[../Week 08/Lecture|Week 08 Lecture]] - current state analysis, personas, customer journey maps.

How to answer:

- For context techniques, choose techniques that reveal different parts of the situation. For example, external pressure, internal capability, stakeholders, process boundaries, or customer experience.
- For each technique, use the pattern: technique -> what it reveals -> why it fits MetroFresh.
- For BA activities, focus on analysis work after planning, not on writing the plan itself.
- Strong activities include current state analysis, stakeholder engagement, customer feedback analysis, process or data assessment, requirements elicitation, feasibility or cost-benefit assessment, and solution option evaluation.
- Pair each activity with suitable elicitation methods such as interviews, workshops, surveys, document analysis, observation, data analysis, or prototype testing.

## Section 2 - Strategy Analysis

### Question 3 - 12 marks

UrbanMove is a public transport service provider. Customers currently contact support through phone calls, emails, and social media messages. Response times are slow, customers repeat the same information across channels, and support staff do not have a central view of each customer's issue history. A senior manager wants UrbanMove to quickly build a mobile self-service support app because "every modern transport provider has one." The manager argues that the team already knows the requirements and should not spend time analysing the current state. The executive team wants a visible improvement before the next annual service review.

Define the business need and business requirement in this scenario. (2 marks)

How should the Business Analyst respond to the senior manager's desire to quickly develop a mobile app without analysing the current state? Include FOUR key arguments in your response. (10 marks)

#### Question 3 Guide

Lecture hints:

- [[../Week 08/Lecture|Week 08 Lecture]] - business needs, current state, POPIT, customer journey maps.
- [[../Week 09/Lecture|Week 09 Lecture]] - business need, business requirement, goals, SMART objectives, constraints, solution scope.
- [[../Week 03/Lecture|Week 03 Lecture]] - context and competitive pressure.
- [[../Week 04/Lecture|Week 04 Lecture]] - stakeholder engagement and elicitation.

How to answer:

- Define the business need as the problem or opportunity that explains why change is required.
- Define the business requirement as the high-level outcome or condition needed to address that need.
- Respond constructively: acknowledge time pressure, then explain why focused current state analysis reduces the risk of building the wrong solution.
- Use four arguments such as: understanding the real need, identifying how current processes and systems operate, understanding stakeholder and channel differences, defining scope and constraints, reducing rework, and producing evidence-based requirements.
- Treat the annual review date as a constraint, not as a reason to skip analysis.

### Question 4 - 13 marks

GreenGrid Solar designs and installs residential solar systems. Customer satisfaction with the finished installations is high, and most customers say they would recommend GreenGrid to others. However, the CEO believes the design and installation process still has serious issues. Sales staff complain that quotes are revised several times, installers report missing site information, and finance staff say profit margins vary widely between similar jobs.

Assuming GreenGrid already achieves high customer satisfaction, how is it still possible that there are issues with the process? Suggest TWO potential issues. (5 marks)

Which technique would you use for identifying each of the two suggested issues, and why? (6 marks)

Once the issues have been identified, how should the Business Analyst prioritise which issues to address first? (2 marks)

#### Question 4 Guide

Lecture hints:

- [[../Week 05/Lecture|Week 05 Lecture]] - problem definition, symptoms, causes, root cause analysis, Five-Why method, fishbone diagram, Pareto analysis.
- [[../Week 08/Lecture|Week 08 Lecture]] - current state analysis and process understanding.
- [[../Week 11/Lecture|Week 11 Lecture]] - measures, metrics, performance variance, solution and enterprise limitations.

How to answer:

- Start by saying customer satisfaction is only one performance perspective. A process can satisfy customers while still being inefficient, costly, risky, inconsistent, or hard for staff to operate.
- Suggest issues that fit the case, such as rework in quote preparation, missing information between sales and installation, inconsistent margins, delays, quality risks, or poor process handoffs.
- Match techniques to the issue: process modelling for handoffs and delays, Five Whys for causal chains, fishbone for multiple cause categories, Pareto analysis for prioritising high-impact causes, or data analysis for margin variance.
- Prioritise using business impact, urgency, frequency, cost, strategic alignment, and Pareto logic.

## Section 3 - Requirements Analysis and Design

### Question 5 - 12 marks

CityAccess Council is planning a digital permit application service for residents and small businesses. The service should allow users to apply for permits, submit supporting documents, pay fees, track application status, and receive decisions online. Council staff will review applications and request further information when needed. The council wants the service to be convenient for users while also meeting privacy, accessibility, and reliability expectations.

Identify THREE functional requirements and THREE non-functional requirements for the CityAccess permit service. For each requirement, briefly explain why it is critical to the success of the service. (12 marks)

#### Question 5 Guide

Lecture hints:

- [[../Week 07/Lecture|Week 07 Lecture]] - requirement types, functional and non-functional requirements, good requirement attributes.
- [[../Week 10/Lecture|Week 10 Lecture]] - requirements classification, specify and model requirements, verify and validate requirements.
- [[../Week 04/Lecture|Week 04 Lecture]] - stakeholders and elicitation.

How to answer:

- Functional requirements describe what the solution must do. Think user actions, staff actions, document handling, payment, status tracking, notifications, and requests for extra information.
- Non-functional requirements describe how well the service must operate. Think accessibility, privacy, security, availability, performance, reliability, auditability, and usability.
- Write requirements as clear, testable statements rather than vague qualities.
- For each requirement, connect the justification to value: user convenience, compliance, staff efficiency, trust, reduced rework, faster decisions, or service accessibility.

### Question 6 - 13 marks

PeoplePlus Retail is implementing an AI-based workforce analytics system across its stores. The system will help HR and store managers analyse recruitment outcomes, staff turnover, training needs, and employee performance trends. As the Business Analyst, you have developed an initial requirements set and are now verifying requirement quality before the requirements are approved.

Provide THREE examples of a good requirement for this project. Briefly explain why each requirement is "good." (6 marks)

Even after the requirements have been verified, explain why a manager may still believe some requirements are not "right." (3 marks)

Identify TWO ethical or cultural problems that could arise from implementing the AI-based workforce analytics system. Briefly explain why each is problematic. (4 marks)

#### Question 6 Guide

Lecture hints:

- [[../Week 07/Lecture|Week 07 Lecture]] - attributes of a good requirement and requirements life cycle management.
- [[../Week 10/Lecture|Week 10 Lecture]] - verification, validation, requirements architecture, design options.
- [[../Week 11/Lecture|Week 11 Lecture]] - cautions about metrics and KPIs, worker measurement risks.
- [[../Week 06/Lecture|Week 06 Lecture]] - BA competencies, value, and fit for purpose.

How to answer:

- Good requirements should be atomic, complete enough, consistent, concise, feasible, unambiguous, testable, prioritised, and understandable for the audience.
- For each example, write a requirement and then name the quality attribute(s) it demonstrates.
- For the verification versus validation part, explain that verified requirements may be well written but still fail to align with the business need, stakeholder need, ethics, scope, or expected value.
- For ethical and cultural risks, consider algorithmic bias, privacy, surveillance, transparency, explainability, employee trust, cultural resistance, and misuse of performance data.

## Section 4 - Solution Evaluation

### Question 7 - 12 marks

FleetPath Logistics has implemented a route optimisation portal for dispatchers and drivers. The portal recommends delivery routes, updates estimated arrival times, and alerts dispatchers to delays. Three months after implementation, fuel costs have fallen, but driver complaints have increased. Some priority deliveries are still late, and customer service staff say customers do not trust the estimated arrival times shown in the portal.

Discuss TWO solution evaluation tasks that would be useful in evaluating the portal. In your discussion, explain why FleetPath would benefit from focusing on those tasks to evaluate and improve the solution. (12 marks)

#### Question 7 Guide

Lecture hints:

- [[../Week 11/Lecture|Week 11 Lecture]] - solution evaluation tasks, performance measures, solution limitations, enterprise limitations, recommendations to increase value.
- [[../Week 05/Lecture|Week 05 Lecture]] - root cause analysis and problem analysis.
- [[../Week 08/Lecture|Week 08 Lecture]] - current state, stakeholders, customer journey maps.

How to answer:

- Choose two formal solution evaluation tasks, not just two metrics.
- Useful tasks include measure solution performance, analyse performance measures, assess solution limitations, assess enterprise limitations, and recommend actions to increase value.
- For each task, define the task briefly, apply it to the case evidence, explain what data or stakeholder input is needed, and state how the task supports a better recommendation.
- A strong answer separates solution limitations from enterprise limitations. For example, inaccurate ETA logic may be a solution limitation, while driver adoption or dispatch process mismatch may be an enterprise limitation.

### Question 8 - 13 marks

SkillBridge is an online professional training provider. It has added a virtual coaching tool to support live classes, breakout discussions, and instructor feedback. Management wants the tool to improve learner engagement and educator productivity. They also want to know whether the tool is improving course outcomes rather than simply increasing online activity.

Define ONE possible measure, TWO possible metrics, and TWO KPIs that should be monitored once the tool is implemented. (9 marks)

Explain why you chose those two KPIs. (4 marks)

#### Question 8 Guide

Lecture hints:

- [[../Week 11/Lecture|Week 11 Lecture]] - measures, metrics, KPIs, good metrics, continuous monitoring, cautions about KPIs.
- [[../Week 09/Lecture|Week 09 Lecture]] - goals, SMART objectives, expected value.
- [[../Week 10/Lecture|Week 10 Lecture]] - validation and value-based recommendation.

How to answer:

- Keep the distinction clear: a measure is a raw value, a metric is derived or interpreted, and a KPI is a strategically important metric linked to objectives.
- Align the KPIs with the stated value: learner engagement, educator productivity, and course outcomes.
- For each metric or KPI, specify what it measures and why it matters. Formula-style wording helps.
- Avoid weak KPIs that only show activity, such as logins, unless you explain how they connect to learning value.
- In the explanation, justify why each KPI is more important than ordinary operational metrics.
