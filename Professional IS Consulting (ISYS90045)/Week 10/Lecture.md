## Key Concepts

### Problem Solving in Consulting

- Consulting problems are often unstructured: they involve economic, technical, organisational, and human factors, and they rarely have one correct answer.
- The consultant's task is to help the client move from ambiguity to a defensible decision.
- Problem solving should combine data analysis, judgement, stakeholder understanding, and practical implementation thinking.
- A good solution is not only analytically correct; it must fit the client's authority, resources, culture, timing, and risk appetite.

### IESE Six-Step Process

- Problem definition: clarify the real issue, separate root causes from symptoms, and define the problem at a scope the decision maker can influence.
- Criteria selection: decide what will be used to compare options, including tangible results, risk, and human or stakeholder effects.
- Alternative development: generate meaningful options that respond to the defined problem.
- Analysis: evaluate alternatives against the selected criteria, using both quantitative and qualitative judgement where needed.
- Decision: choose an option after weighing evidence, trade-offs, and constraints.
- Action plan creation: convert the decision into steps, responsibilities, sequencing, resources, and follow-up.

### Problem-Solving Style and Fit

- Different people approach problems differently. Some prefer structure, established methods, and incremental improvement; others prefer novelty, reframing, and more radical change.
- In consulting, style matters because a method that fits one problem or client may frustrate another.
- Effective teams benefit from style diversity if they can manage disagreement and use different perspectives deliberately.
- Fit matters between consultant, client, task, and phase of work. Early diagnosis may need divergent thinking; implementation may need discipline and adaptation.

### Linking Problem Solving to Advice

- Problem solving should produce a recommendation the client can act on.
- Criteria should be set before alternatives are favoured to reduce bias.
- Analysis should be deep enough to support the decision but not so broad that it delays action unnecessarily.
- The final action plan is part of the solution, not an optional afterthought.

### Reading Notes

- Rosenberg distinguishes structured problems from unstructured business problems, where solutions and outcomes are uncertain.
- His six-step process provides a disciplined way to move from problem definition to action while balancing analysis and judgement.
- Summers, Sweeney, and Wolk focus attention on problem-solving style and fit in consulting and auditing. The implication for consulting is that capability is not only individual skill but also alignment between style, task, and context.
- Together, the readings support a practical view: define the problem carefully, choose criteria before options, analyse with awareness of bias, and translate decisions into implementable action.

## Examples

- A client may frame low adoption as a training problem, but problem definition might reveal incentive, workflow, interface, or leadership issues.
- A criteria set for a system recommendation might include cost, implementation risk, user impact, scalability, regulatory compliance, and time to value.
- A technically elegant option may be rejected if it is too risky, too slow, or outside the client's capability to maintain.

## Questions

- Why is problem definition often the hardest step?
- How does setting criteria before alternatives reduce bias?
- What kinds of problems need structured analysis, and what kinds need reframing?
- How can a consultant build an action plan that is realistic for the client?

## Summary

Problem solving in consulting is disciplined movement from ambiguity to action. The week connects structured problem-solving processes with the need to match problem-solving style to the client, task, and implementation context.
