## Course-Sourced Material

### Problem Solving in Consulting

- Consulting problems are often unstructured: they involve economic, technical, organisational, and human factors, and they rarely have one correct answer.
- The consultant's task is to help the client move from ambiguity to a defensible decision.
- Problem solving should combine data analysis, judgement, stakeholder understanding, and practical implementation thinking.
- A good solution is not only analytically correct; it must fit the client's authority, resources, culture, timing, and risk appetite.
- Information systems consulting problems may include cloud migration, security improvement, software fit, IT roadmapping, outsourcing, data loss prevention, major IT uplift, or implementing a chosen platform.
- Decision making is part of problem solving because judgement calls are required while defining, analysing, selecting, and implementing a solution.

### Structured and Unstructured Problems

| Dimension | Structured problems | Unstructured problems |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Decision type | Programmed | Non-programmed |
| Frequency | Repetitive or routine | New, unusual, or ambiguous |
| Goals | Clear and specific | Vague or contested |
| Information | Available and sufficient | Missing, unclear, or incomplete |
| Consequences | Usually minor | Often major |
| Organisational level | Lower-level operational decisions | Senior-level or strategic decisions |
| Basis for solution | Rules, procedures, or SOPs | Judgement, creativity, and evidence |
| Evaluation | Defined criteria and process | No definite criteria or stopping rule |

- Structured problems have a clear goal, beginning state, allowable actions, end state, and constraints.
- Structured problems can often be repeated through a documented process.
- Unstructured problems are open-ended and may involve unknown problem elements, multiple solution paths, uncertain rules, missing information, and defensible judgement.
- Semi-structured problems sit between these categories: some parts can be proceduralised, but other parts still require judgement.

### IESE Six-Step Process

- Problem definition: clarify the real issue, separate root causes from symptoms, and define the problem at a scope the decision maker can influence.
- Criteria selection: decide what will be used to compare options, including tangible results, risk, and human or stakeholder effects.
- Alternative development: generate meaningful options that respond to the defined problem.
- Analysis: evaluate alternatives against the selected criteria, using both quantitative and qualitative judgement where needed.
- Decision: choose an option after weighing evidence, trade-offs, and constraints.
- Action plan creation: convert the decision into steps, responsibilities, sequencing, resources, and follow-up.
- For this subject and assessments, Rosenberg's model is the main unstructured problem-solving model to apply.
- The process is not just a report format. It is a discipline for making trade-offs visible before recommending a course of action.
- Criteria should be selected before alternatives are developed because pre-selecting options can bias the criteria toward a preferred answer.

### Three-Stage Problem-Solving Cycle

- Understand the problem.
  - Ask what is being sought, what is unknown, whether all concepts and measurements are understood, what information is missing, what information is irrelevant, and whether the source is reliable.
  - The lecture emphasised that misunderstanding the problem makes the later work ineffective even if the analysis is technically careful.
- Strategise.
  - Choose one or more strategies suited to the problem.
  - General strategies include logical reasoning, pattern recognition, working backwards, adopting a different point of view, considering extreme cases, solving a simpler analogous problem, organising data, making a visual representation, accounting for all possibilities, and intelligent guessing and testing.
- Implement.
  - Apply the strategy while checking reasoning, calculations, and assumptions.
  - Implementation can refine understanding, which may require refining the strategy and approach.

### Problem-Solving Style and Fit

- Different people approach problems differently. Some prefer structure, established methods, and incremental improvement; others prefer novelty, reframing, and more radical change.
- In consulting, style matters because a method that fits one problem or client may frustrate another.
- Effective teams benefit from style diversity if they can manage disagreement and use different perspectives deliberately.
- Fit matters between consultant, client, task, and phase of work. Early diagnosis may need divergent thinking; implementation may need discipline and adaptation.
- The lecture connected consulting work to collective cognition: teams can layer meaning, perspectives, and experience to solve problems that one individual may frame too narrowly.
- Diverse consulting teams can reduce confirmation bias and groupthink when members feel safe to challenge assumptions.
- The lecture also linked problem solving to leadership skills: problem framing, empathy, breaking cognitive fixedness, and creating a psychologically safe environment.

### Problem Attributes

- Domain: the content area and concepts that define the problem, such as information systems or information security.
- Type: the related concepts and rules that shape how the problem can be solved, such as security risk management.
- Process: the method used to move from the initial state to the goal state.
- Solution: the achieved outcome, which may be convergent with one right answer or divergent with several acceptable answers.

### Tame and Wicked Problems

- Tame problems can be solved or brought under control through a defined method.
- Wicked problems can only be worked on because they have no definitive formulation, no clear stopping rule, no true-or-false solution, no immediate final test, and no complete list of possible solutions.
- Wicked problems are unique, can be symptoms of other problems, and make the planner responsible for consequences even when certainty is impossible.
- In consulting, the distinction matters because some client issues should be solved through project delivery, while broader social, organisational, or strategic tensions may need ongoing management rather than a final answer.

### Linking Problem Solving to Advice

- Problem solving should produce a recommendation the client can act on.
- Criteria should be set before alternatives are favoured to reduce bias.
- Analysis should be deep enough to support the decision but not so broad that it delays action unnecessarily.
- The final action plan is part of the solution, not an optional afterthought.
- A proposal presentation should make the client's problem explicit, show the main causes, and explain how the proposed solution addresses those causes.
- The lecture recommends using no more than three main causes when presenting a problem to a client, so the logic stays clear.
- Evidence matters: reports, examples, demonstrations, relevant experience, CVs, testimonials, and credentials help the client trust that the consultant can solve the problem.

### Reading Notes

- Rosenberg distinguishes structured problems from unstructured business problems, where solutions and outcomes are uncertain.
- His six-step process provides a disciplined way to move from problem definition to action while balancing analysis and judgement.
- Rosenberg argues that the best managers pursue tangible results, organisational learning, and a larger sense of purpose when dealing with unstructured problems.
- Rosenberg stresses scope in problem definition: a useful problem statement should be framed so the decision maker has authority and access to act.
- Rosenberg's criteria selection step should include tangible results, risk, and effects on human stakeholders such as customers, employees, suppliers, or other people affected by the decision.
- Rosenberg's analysis step may combine numerical evaluation with subjective judgement. A selection grid can make the relative attractiveness of options visible.
- Rosenberg's action plan step should maximise the positives and mitigate the negatives identified during analysis.
- Summers, Sweeney, and Wolk focus attention on problem-solving style and fit in consulting and auditing. The implication for consulting is that capability is not only individual skill but also alignment between style, task, and context.
- The lecture uses Summers, Sweeney, and Wolk to support the point that consultants tend to be more inclined toward creative and original ideas and can function effectively in unstructured settings.
- Summers, Sweeney, and Wolk found that consultants were, on average, more innovative in problem-solving style than auditors.
- Their abstract-level findings link poor fit between preferred problem-solving style and job demands to higher role stress, lower job satisfaction, weaker organisational commitment, and reduced intent to stay with the firm.
- For consulting practice, this supports staffing and team-design decisions: match people to the problem type, and use mixed styles deliberately when the work requires both creativity and implementation discipline.
- Together, the readings support a practical view: define the problem carefully, choose criteria before options, analyse with awareness of bias, and translate decisions into implementable action.

## Examples

- A client may frame low adoption as a training problem, but problem definition might reveal incentive, workflow, interface, or leadership issues.
- A criteria set for a system recommendation might include cost, implementation risk, user impact, scalability, regulatory compliance, and time to value.
- A technically elegant option may be rejected if it is too risky, too slow, or outside the client's capability to maintain.
- A client asking whether to acquire a company requires unstructured analysis: options could include full acquisition, minority investment, joint venture, or no acquisition.
- A technology refresh proposal should distinguish deliverables from outcomes. A deliverable might be an app, system, prototype, report, or implementation plan; an outcome might be reduced manual work, improved adoption, higher satisfaction, or better service quality.
