## Key Concepts

### Hypothesis-Driven Consulting

- Hypothesis-driven consulting begins with a testable explanation or answer, then gathers evidence to validate, refine, or reject it.
- The aim is not to force the evidence to fit the first answer. The aim is to focus analysis so the team learns quickly.
- A hypothesis gives structure to ambiguous problems by defining what would need to be true for a recommendation to hold.
- Hypotheses should be explicit, testable, linked to evidence, and updated as new information appears.
- A hypothesis is an idea or theory based on limited data. It starts a thread of further investigation rather than closing the question.
- The lecture compared hypothesis-driven work to detective work: the consultant follows possible explanations, tests them, and learns from paths that do not work.
- A hypothesis is more than an educated guess because it is informed by expertise, background information, preliminary analysis, and subject matter expert input.
- The hypothesis can also be described as a consultant's point of view, perspective, or value proposition, but it must stay open to correction.
- Hypothesis-driven consulting is not the same as assuming the answer at the start. Treating the first idea as the answer creates the "consultant know-it-all" risk.

### Problem Framing

- Start by clarifying the decision the client needs to make.
- Define the problem tightly enough that analysis can be directed.
- Separate symptoms from possible causes.
- Identify constraints such as time, budget, authority, data access, stakeholder appetite, and implementation capacity.
- Hypotheses help open the problem by asking "what if" questions and exploring possible causal paths.
- A client symptom, such as slow invoice payment or falling market share, may have system, process, people, data, training, governance, or customer-input causes.
- Consultants should avoid mistaking correlation for causation. A visible symptom may be associated with a problem without being the true cause.

### Analysis Structure

- Break large problems into smaller questions.
- Use issue trees or logic trees to identify drivers, causes, options, and evidence needs.
- Prioritise analysis by impact and uncertainty. Do not analyse everything equally.
- Use evidence to disconfirm weak hypotheses early.
- The slide process moves through problem definition, problem structuring, analysis, prioritisation, hypothesis development, work planning, deeper analysis, and solution development.
- Hypotheses inform the fact base: what data to collect, which interviews to run, what process or system evidence to inspect, and which analysis will be useful.
- The overall cycle is iterative: set goals, form a hypothesis, test content, analyse results, and refine the hypothesis.
- Prioritisation matters because clients rarely have time or budget to test every idea. Consultants should focus first on hypotheses with high potential impact or high uncertainty.
- Validation can use logic, observation, testing, surveys, system data, interviews, and analysis.
- A useful hypothesis statement can follow the pattern: "We believe that creating this feature for these people will achieve this outcome. We will validate this when we see this result affect the business in this way."

### Communication

- A hypothesis-driven message often uses a top-down structure: answer first, then supporting reasons, then evidence.
- This structure is useful for executive communication because it makes the recommendation and logic clear.
- Consultants should still show enough reasoning for the client to trust the recommendation.
- When a hypothesis changes, explain why the evidence changed the view.
- Clients may initially be uncomfortable with hypothesis-driven work because it can look like the consultant is asking obvious questions or rushing to a provisional answer.
- Consultants should explain that hypotheses are working assumptions used to focus learning, not fixed conclusions.
- The client should be involved in developing and revising hypotheses. The guest consultant described hypothesis-based problem solving as co-design rather than isolated expert work.
- If the client identifies a better solution during the engagement, that is not a failure of the method. It means the hypothesis process has helped surface a better path.

### Good and Poor Practice

- Good practice includes using all available context, involving the team and client, testing constantly, and revising as new information appears.
- Do not wait until every possible fact is available; the engagement may have fixed timeframes and incomplete data.
- Do not waste time perfecting an early hypothesis or its supporting analysis before testing whether it is useful.
- Poor practice includes failing to question the hypothesis, defending it blindly, or adjusting data to fit the preferred answer.

### Guest Consultant Notes

- The guest consultant framed consulting work as helping organisations answer questions they lack the time, funding, manpower, or skill set to solve internally.
- A common consulting path starts with an RFQ or client problem, moves through proposal and selection, then into strategy, current-state analysis, future-state design, roadmap development, and sometimes implementation.
- Strategy work is often high-level and business-oriented, while later work may examine operating models, governance, enterprise architecture, data, systems, and transformation delivery.
- Enterprise architecture work can map current systems, future architecture, and a roadmap for uplift across systems, data, infrastructure, security, and organisational processes.
- Consulting experience builds skill in handling hard situations, ambiguous problems, high-pressure client environments, and finding answers when no one already knows the answer.
- A change request may be needed when new information changes scope, timeline, resourcing, or cost.

### Reading Notes

- Kasper's reading frames hypothesis-driven work as a practical consulting discipline associated with McKinsey-style problem solving.
- The central reading takeaway is to avoid unfocused analysis by starting with a provisional answer and testing it.
- Hypothesis-driven work is strongest when the team treats hypotheses as learning tools, not as positions to defend.
- This approach connects to earlier proposal and advice topics: good consultants state the problem, choose an analytical path, and communicate the logic behind their advice.

## Examples

- Instead of asking "Why are sales down?", a consultant might test: "Sales are down because conversion has fallen in the online channel after checkout changes."
- Evidence could then be gathered on traffic, conversion, cart abandonment, customer complaints, release dates, and competitor activity.
- If evidence disproves the hypothesis, the consultant should update the issue tree rather than defend the original view.
- For slow invoice payment, possible hypotheses could include ERP workflow delays, batch processing, missing approval authority, part-time approvers, poor training, missing purchase order data, or budget-check access problems.
- For low website conversion, hypotheses could relate to visual design, confusing navigation, poor mobile experience, weak search visibility, slow performance, irrelevant content, or low trust signals.
- For staff turnover rising from 2% to 10%, hypotheses could include leadership change, pay, workload, procedures, culture, career progression, or labour market competition.

## Summary

Hypothesis-driven consulting gives structure to ambiguous client problems. It focuses analysis, supports faster learning, and improves communication, provided the consultant remains evidence-led and willing to revise the initial view. The Week 9 slides and transcript emphasise that a hypothesis is a living, testable starting point: it should guide evidence collection, not become a fixed answer to defend.
