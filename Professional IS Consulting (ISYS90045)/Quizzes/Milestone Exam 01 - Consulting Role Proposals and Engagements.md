# Milestone Exam 01 - Consulting Role Proposals and Engagements

Course: [[Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)/Course Overview|Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)]]
Coverage: [[Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)/Week 01/Lecture|Week 01]], [[Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)/Week 02/Lecture|Week 02]], [[Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)/Week 03/Lecture|Week 03]]
Suggested timing: 60 minutes, closed book
Marks: 40

## Scope

This milestone exam checks consulting role, firm types, professional conduct, proposal purpose and structure, audience analysis, bid/no-bid judgement, and the engagement life cycle.

## Section A - Short Answers

Each question is worth 2 marks.

1. Why do clients commonly hire consultants?
2. Why is IS consulting broader than technical advice?
3. Distinguish external consultants from internal consultants.
4. Explain one risk of vendor consulting.
5. Name three consulting capabilities beyond subject matter expertise.
6. Distinguish an executive summary from an introduction in a proposal.
7. Explain why "understanding of needs" is a critical proposal section.
8. Distinguish an RFI, RFQ, RFT, and RFP.
9. Name the main phases of the consulting engagement life cycle.
10. Explain why uncontrolled scope change creates consulting risk.

## Section B - Applied Questions

Each question is worth 5 marks.

11. A client asks for a new system. Explain what a consultant should diagnose before accepting that technology is the right answer.
12. A proposal repeats the RFP wording but does not interpret the client's problem. Explain why this is weak.
13. A boutique consultancy considers bidding for a tender that requires extensive prior public-sector experience it does not have. Explain the bid/no-bid reasoning.
14. A proposal promises extra workshops, training, and support without pricing or assumptions. Explain the delivery risk.

## Section C - Scenario

This question is worth 10 marks.

15. A mid-sized manufacturer asks three consultancies to submit proposals for an ERP review. The CEO wants speed and cost certainty, the CIO wants technical detail, procurement wants compliance with the RFP, and operations managers worry that consultants will not understand daily workflows.

Explain how a strong consulting proposal should address audience, client need, methodology, assumptions, pricing, evidence of similar work, and later engagement risk.
