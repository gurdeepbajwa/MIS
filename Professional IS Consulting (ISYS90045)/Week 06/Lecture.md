## Key Concepts

### Negotiation in the Engagement Life Cycle

- Negotiation appears during contract formation, but it also appears throughout delivery in scope, resources, milestones, responsibilities, change requests, and stakeholder expectations.
- Contractual negotiation clarifies what is included, excluded, priced, timed, and owned.
- Negotiation should protect both the client outcome and the consultant's ability to deliver responsibly.
- The transcript emphasised that negotiation also happens before formal consulting work, including job offers, salary, group work, resource allocation, timing, and internal team commitments.
- Scope creep is a negotiation problem when training, support, extra workshops, travel, or additional deliverables were never clearly included.

### What Negotiation Is

- Negotiation is a deliberative process between two or more actors seeking a solution to a shared issue or bargaining over something of value.
- Parties bring their own wants, needs, interests, constraints, and alternatives.
- Negotiation skill includes both creating value and claiming value.
- Consulting negotiation should aim to make deals, solve problems, manage conflict, and preserve relationships.

### Preparation

- Know the bottom line before negotiating: the minimum acceptable price, maximum acceptable concession, non-negotiable scope boundary, or walk-away condition.
- Identify floors and ceilings, likely questions, alternative options, and what you will say if the request is unreasonable.
- Prepare for the hardest questions, especially those about price, competing offers, scope exclusions, timelines, capability, and risk.
- Be ready to say no clearly and professionally. Saying no can protect trust when yes would create overpromise and underdelivery.
- A respectful refusal can preserve trust: "we cannot do it for that amount; if circumstances change, please contact us" is better than accepting work that cannot be delivered.
- Hard questions should not be answered by inventing leverage or disclosing confidential information. The consultant should be honest, redirect to value, or clarify the underlying concern.
- Decide in advance what is negotiable, what is not negotiable, and what the firm cannot do because of law, policy, ethics, confidentiality, or professional standards.

### Negotiation Styles

- Open-book negotiation requires enough trust for both parties to share constraints, cost logic, and priorities.
- Take-it-or-leave-it negotiation relies on power imbalance and can be problematic in advisory relationships.
- Ambit claims add demands mainly as leverage, but in consulting they can look opportunistic and damage trust.
- Best and final offer (BAFO) processes are common in tenders and require clarity on final terms, price, and scope.

### Trust, Emotion, and Neutrality

- Trust matters because negotiation outcomes often depend on whether parties believe each other's intentions and constraints.
- Overpromising can win a point in the short term while damaging delivery and future trust.
- Emotion, body language, and perceived respect shape the negotiation dynamic.
- Neutrality is partly behavioural: posture, gestures, tone, timing, and responses can escalate or calm a negotiation.
- Low trust increases cost because each claim needs more proof, each concession is treated defensively, and conversation becomes slower.
- Saying no does not automatically erode trust. Saying yes dishonestly, then underdelivering, does.
- Anger can destroy respect and force others to de-escalate instead of solving the problem.
- Language such as "we" can frame negotiation as a shared problem rather than a battle between sides.
- Gesture, seating, handshake, posture, and tone can become power moves even when the words sound neutral.

### Reading Notes

- Shonk argues that negotiation matters because it helps people make deals, manage conflict, build relationships, and advance careers.
- The reading aligns with the lecture's emphasis on preparation, relationship preservation, and careful handling of opening positions.
- Poor opening offers, public missteps, hardball tactics, and low-trust bargaining can create reputational harm even when one party has power.
- Effective negotiation is not just getting the lowest price or strongest terms. In consulting, the negotiated agreement must still support delivery, trust, and value.
- Fisher and Ury's principled negotiation approach shifts attention from positional haggling to solving the problem in a way that protects the relationship.
- Negotiators should pay attention to both the substance of the deal and the process by which the deal is reached.
- Emotions and perceptions matter because both sides are people, not purely rational transaction machines.
- Active listening and reframing can reduce defensiveness by showing that the other side has been heard before the consultant advances their own position.
- Objective standards, benchmarks, regulations, precedents, and measurable criteria can make negotiation feel fairer and less personal.
- A plan B or walk-away option improves negotiation power and reduces desperation; a bad agreement can be worse than no agreement.

## Examples

- A client asks for extra workshops within the same fixed fee. A consultant can negotiate by explaining the impact on timeline, budget, or deliverable depth and offering options.
- A lowball opening offer may offend the other party and make later agreement harder.
- A consultant should avoid accepting an unrealistic timeline simply to win the work, because delivery failure will damage trust.
- A client may ask for discriminatory staffing conditions. The consultant should refuse clearly because the request conflicts with law and professional standards.
- Travel can be negotiated by reframing the underlying need: if the client wants effective engagement but not travel cost, remote workshops may be a workable option.

## Summary

Negotiation is a recurring consulting skill, not a one-off contract activity. Good negotiation requires preparation, clarity about limits, trust awareness, emotional control, and the discipline to reach agreements that can actually be delivered.
