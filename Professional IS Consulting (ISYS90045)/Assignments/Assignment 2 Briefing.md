## Briefing Overview

Assignment 2 uses one case study for both the group presentation and the written proposal. The group should present as a consultancy responding to the client's request for proposal, not as students summarising a case.

The briefing emphasises:

- choose one of the three case studies and use it consistently across AT2a and AT2b;
- be clear about what the client is asking for;
- identify the political, organisational, and technical complexities in the case;
- identify the actors, likely decision makers, and actual client;
- identify missing information and state defensible assumptions where the case does not provide enough detail.

## Case Study Options

### Austech Manufacturing Alliance

- Network of nine mid-sized manufacturing plants across Victoria and South Australia.
- The client needs an implementation partner for a unified ERP system across all plants.
- Likely complexity: multi-site standardisation, old and inconsistent systems, production disruption, stakeholder resistance, data integration, and energy or emissions reporting.

### Harper Fitness

- Global chain of gyms and wellness centres affected by digital competitors and fitness influencers.
- The client wants a technology refresh to modernise its digital presence.
- Likely complexity: website and app refresh, digital engagement, membership experience, class booking, chatbot or support channels, data integration, and adoption by staff and members.

### BrightMart

- Mid-sized supermarket and convenience retail chain operating across Australia, New Zealand, and Singapore.
- The client faces sharp grocery price increases and wants a technology refresh program across discovery, inception, build, testing, and implementation.
- Likely complexity: price pressure, competition from larger retailers and digital platforms, customer trust, cultural resistance to technology, loyalty, stock, pricing, and digital retail capability.

## Presentation Requirements

The presentation should be a cohesive consulting pitch rather than five separate mini-presentations.

Key points from the briefing:

- all group members should speak;
- the group should look and sound like one consultancy team;
- slides and other AV material should support the presentation, not distract from it;
- key information should appear early, especially price, timeline, and deliverables;
- the client should quickly understand what will be delivered, what it will cost, how long it will take, and what benefits are expected.

Presentation marking areas:

| Area | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| Presentation and organisation | Professional standard, clear message, appropriate use of AV, timing, and audience fit. |
| Understanding of problem | Clear statement of the client's problem from the client's perspective. |
| Deliverables and outcomes | Clear explanation of what will be delivered and what benefits the client should expect. |
| Schedule | Reasonable and relevant timeframe. |
| Resources | People and technical resources with relevant experience. |
| Governance | Selected governance structure and decision-making approach. |
| Pricing | Pricing structure and commercial logic. |
| Risk management | Risk framework, major risks, and mitigation. |
| Industry insights | Credentials, capability, similar work, or other evidence that builds client confidence. |

## Written Proposal Requirements

The written proposal should use the same case study as the presentation and should be written as a professional consultancy proposal.

Expected structure:

- Executive summary
- Introduction
- Our understanding of your needs
- Engagement methodology and timelines
- Pricing and assumptions
- Team CVs
- Similar work we have performed

The executive summary should summarise the full proposal rather than introduce the report.

## Deliverables and Outcomes

The briefing separates deliverables from outcomes:

- Deliverables are what the consultancy gives the client, such as a report, prototype, system, roadmap, implementation plan, training package, or deployed solution.
- Outcomes are the benefits that result if the client uses the deliverables, such as improved productivity, reduced manual work, higher customer satisfaction, better adoption, lower service burden, or stronger digital engagement.

A strong proposal should make both visible.

## Governance, Risk, and Pricing

Governance should explain how the project will be controlled when timeline, budget, scope, or quality goes off track. The briefing stresses that governance requires people with decision-making authority, not only delivery team members.

Risk should focus on the major risks that could derail the project. A probability-impact matrix can be used, but the client-facing writing should explain the risk, consequence, mitigation, owner, and escalation path rather than over-explaining the model.

Pricing should show where the costs go. Consulting labour is usually the largest cost, so the proposal should explain roles, rates or effort, timeline, and major technology or implementation costs. Return on investment helps justify a large technology spend as an investment rather than only an expense.

## Common Pitfalls

- Waiting too long to reveal price, timeline, or deliverables.
- Presenting as disconnected individuals rather than one consulting team.
- Explaining generic models instead of explaining what they mean for the client.
- Skipping criteria the client asked for.
- Calling an introduction an executive summary.
- Using governance as a project-team chart rather than a decision and escalation structure.
- Forgetting references for external sources, market research, professional sources, or AI tools.
- Treating missing case information as a blocker rather than making clear and defensible assumptions.

## Practical Writing Notes

- Use tables, diagrams, appendices, and concise wording to manage the word limit.
- State the selected approach directly, then explain what it means for the client.
- Use creative licence carefully for credentials, testimonials, and similar work, but keep them plausible and relevant to the selected case.
- Declare AI use honestly if it supports grammar, calculations, research, or drafting.
- Rehearse the presentation as a group so handovers, timing, and message coherence are controlled.
