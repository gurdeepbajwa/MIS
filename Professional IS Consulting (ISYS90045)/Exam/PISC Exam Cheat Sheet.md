# PISC Exam Cheat Sheet

Course: [[Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)/Course Overview|Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)]]
Last updated: 2026-06-02
Purpose: fast revision before practice questions.

## Core Exam Rule

PISC exam answers are not definition dumps. The strongest answers:

1. identify the consulting issue being tested;
2. name the relevant concept or framework;
3. define it briefly;
4. apply it to the specific scenario facts;
5. explain the consulting risk, relationship issue, delivery implication, or trade-off;
6. recommend the next professional action.

Use course language: `proposal`, `engagement`, `scope`, `assumption`, `deliverable`, `stakeholder`, `sponsor`, `advice`, `risk`, `finding`, `recommendation`, `governance`, `sign-off`.

## Scenario Trigger Map

| If the scenario says... | Use these frameworks |
| --- | --- |
| The client asks the firm to bid or respond to an RFP | Proposal structure, audience analysis, bid/no-bid judgement, engagement life cycle |
| The client wants a fixed low price, fast timeline, or "extra work if there is time" | Negotiation, scope control, assumptions, governance, risk |
| The client has a messy or unclear problem | Structured versus unstructured problems, IESE six-step process, hypothesis-driven consulting |
| The consultant is collecting evidence from stakeholders | Meetings, interviews, workshops, stakeholder selection, social cues, evidence trail |
| The client needs a recommendation | Professional advice, evidence, options, trade-offs, assumptions, actionability |
| Stakeholders resist, argue, or distrust the work | Conflict type, causes of conflict, difficult conversations, trust, escalation |
| Delivery is ending or disputed | Close-out, sign-off, handover, residual risks, lessons learned |
| The question asks why a method matters | Explain the risk of skipping it and the effect on trust, evidence, scope, delivery, or client action |

## Ten Framework Headings

### 1. Consulting Role

Consultants help organisations solve problems they cannot, should not, or do not want to solve alone.

Key points:

- hired for specialist expertise, external perspective, temporary capacity, credibility, or structured delivery methods;
- not only technical experts: IS consulting can involve strategy, architecture, change, business analysis, governance, testing, project delivery, and stakeholder communication;
- clients buy trust before they buy advice;
- consultants work with ambiguity and incomplete information;
- professionalism includes reliability, listening, punctuality, tone, preparation, client focus, and conduct.

Exam sentence:

> A consultant's role is to bring expertise, structure, independence, and professional judgement to help the client diagnose the issue, evaluate options, and act, while managing trust, stakeholder needs, ambiguity, and delivery risk.

Common mistake: saying the consultant simply "fixes the problem". The consultant usually helps diagnose, advise, structure, deliver, or support; the client still owns decisions and organisational change.

### 2. Proposal

A proposal is a written offer that explains the client's problem, the services offered, the approach, budget, team, assumptions, and evidence of similar work.

Proposal request types:

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| RFI | Request for information; explores what is available in the market. |
| EOI | Expression of interest; checks supplier interest before a later proposal process. |
| RFQ | Request for quote; pricing-focused, often for standardised services. |
| RFT | Request for tender; formal bid request, common in government. |
| RFP | Request for proposal; private-sector-style request for a proposed solution. |

Basic proposal structure:

- executive summary: decision-focused summary, not just an introduction;
- introduction: background, scope, structure, and why the consultant is suited;
- understanding of needs: interpretation of the client's real issue;
- methodology and timeline: how the work will be governed, sequenced, and delivered;
- pricing and assumptions: cost, exclusions, limits, and budget logic;
- team CVs: who will do the work and what expertise they bring;
- similar work: relevant experience and outcomes.

Exam sentence:

> A strong proposal proves that the consultant understands the client's real need, can deliver credibly, and has made scope, method, timeline, pricing, assumptions, risks, and responsibilities explicit.

Common mistake: treating the proposal as only a sales pitch. It also sets delivery expectations and can create later scope, pricing, governance, and close-out risks.

### 3. Engagement Life Cycle

The engagement life cycle explains how consulting work is assessed, won, contracted, delivered, and closed.

| Stage | What happens | Main risk if weak |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Risk assessment and internal inquiry | Decide whether to pursue the work; check capability, client reputation, reliance, and delivery risk. | The firm accepts work it cannot safely or profitably deliver. |
| Winning the work | Understand the request, assemble the bid team, design the response, review the proposal, prepare for questions. | Proposal overpromises, misses mandatory criteria, or misunderstands the client. |
| Contractual negotiation | Confirm scope, budget, legal clauses, responsibilities, timelines, IP, confidentiality, and sign-off. | The parties later disagree about what was included. |
| Delivery and project management | Kick-off, perform the work, manage tasks, budget, communication, governance, and method. | Scope creep, weak updates, unresolved risks, or stakeholder loss of trust. |
| Close-out and quality assurance | Final review, legal/quality checks, final presentation, iterations, sign-off, handover. | Deliverables are not accepted, invoicing is delayed, or unresolved issues damage the relationship. |

Exam sentence:

> The life cycle matters because early proposal and contract decisions shape later delivery, governance, client expectations, and close-out.

Common mistake: saying it is just "the cycle of working with a client". For marks, name the stages and explain the risk controlled by each stage.

### 4. Professional Advice

Professional advice is structured judgement in response to a client problem, not just an opinion.

Advice should include:

- client objective;
- evidence and analysis;
- options;
- trade-offs;
- assumptions and limits;
- risks and dependencies;
- clear recommendation;
- practical next steps.

Information versus analysis versus advice:

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Information | Facts, observations, or data. |
| Analysis | What the information means. |
| Advice | What the client should do, based on analysis and judgement. |

Finding versus recommendation:

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Finding | What the consultant discovered. |
| Recommendation | What the consultant says the client should do about it. |

Exam sentence:

> Good professional advice turns evidence into an actionable recommendation by making the reasoning, trade-offs, assumptions, limits, and consequences clear enough for the client to decide.

Common mistake: giving lots of facts without making the judgement explicit.

### 5. Negotiation

Negotiation is a deliberative process where two or more parties seek agreement over something of value.

In consulting, negotiation can involve:

- scope;
- price;
- timeline;
- deliverables;
- responsibilities;
- assumptions;
- resources;
- change requests;
- sign-off;
- extra workshops, training, travel, support, or implementation work.

Before negotiating, know:

- bottom line;
- walk-away condition;
- what is negotiable;
- what is non-negotiable;
- likely hard questions;
- objective criteria or benchmarks;
- the relationship impact of saying yes or no.

Exam sentence:

> Consulting negotiation should align interests, scope, price, time, quality, and responsibilities so the agreement can actually be delivered while preserving trust.

Common mistake: saying negotiation is only about finding a win-win deal. In exam answers, explain scope, constraints, alternatives, limits, and delivery consequences.

### 6. Meetings, Interviews, And Workshops

These are different consulting tools, not interchangeable formats.

| Tool | Best for | Watch for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Meeting | Governance, decisions, progress, risk, alignment, shared updates. | Too many people, unclear purpose, no decision, no follow-up. |
| Interview | Depth, stakeholder perspective, evidence, assumptions, hidden issues. | Leading questions, poor notes, no safety for honest answers. |
| Workshop | Shared understanding, collaboration, prioritisation, design, problem solving. | Power dynamics, poor facilitation, vague outputs. |

Meeting essentials:

- clear purpose;
- relevant participants;
- agenda;
- prepared materials;
- time discipline;
- visible actions;
- minutes or agreed actions sent promptly.

Interview essentials:

- open, closed, and probing questions;
- active listening;
- social cues;
- evidence trail;
- distinction between observation, opinion, consultant interpretation, and follow-up needs.

Workshop essentials:

- objective;
- activities;
- timing;
- participant plan;
- outputs;
- write-up.

Exam sentence:

> Meetings, interviews, and workshops gather evidence, shape trust, align stakeholders, test assumptions, and support decisions; the consultant must choose the format that fits the evidence and relationship need.

Common mistake: saying they are just used to "engage clients". That is true but not enough.

### 7. Problem Solving

Consulting problems are often unstructured. The consultant helps the client move from ambiguity to a defensible decision.

Structured versus unstructured:

| Structured | Unstructured |
| --- | --- |
| Routine or repetitive. | New, unusual, ambiguous. |
| Clear goals and rules. | Vague or contested goals. |
| Sufficient information. | Missing or incomplete information. |
| Often solved through procedures. | Requires judgement, creativity, and evidence. |

IESE six-step process:

1. Problem definition: clarify the real issue, not just the symptom.
2. Criteria selection: decide how options will be judged.
3. Alternative development: generate meaningful options.
4. Analysis: evaluate options against the criteria.
5. Decision: choose after weighing evidence and trade-offs.
6. Action plan: define steps, responsibilities, timing, resources, and follow-up.

Tame versus wicked:

- tame problems can be solved or controlled through a defined method;
- wicked problems have no definitive formulation, no clear stopping rule, and no simple true-or-false solution.

Exam sentence:

> For an unstructured consulting problem, the consultant should first define the real issue, set criteria, develop alternatives, analyse trade-offs, make a defensible decision, and convert it into an action plan.

Common mistake: jumping straight to a solution before problem definition and criteria.

### 8. Risk And Governance

Risk is uncertainty that may affect objectives. Governance defines how decisions, responsibilities, reporting, escalation, and approvals work.

Risk can involve:

- delivery;
- scope;
- cost;
- schedule;
- quality;
- stakeholder acceptance;
- legal or compliance exposure;
- data, security, or technical feasibility;
- relationship and reputation;
- unclear ownership.

Governance includes:

- decision rights;
- roles and responsibilities;
- status reporting;
- steering committee or project board;
- delivery stand-ups;
- project management forum;
- issue and risk registers;
- escalation paths;
- sign-off authority.

Risk-register answer pattern:

| Element | What to write |
| --- | --- |
| Risk | What might happen. |
| Evidence | Scenario fact that shows the risk. |
| Consequence | Why it matters. |
| Mitigation | What should be done. |
| Owner/escalation | Who should manage or decide it. |

Exam sentence:

> Governance makes risk manageable by assigning decision rights, ownership, reporting, and escalation paths so issues are visible before they damage scope, cost, timing, quality, or trust.

Common mistake: defining risk only as "not fixing the client's problem". Exam risk is broader and includes risks created by the consulting engagement itself.

### 9. Conflict

Conflict is friction or disagreement where the beliefs or actions of one person or group are resisted by another.

Types:

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Intragroup conflict | Conflict inside the same team or group. |
| Intergroup conflict | Conflict between groups, such as IT and business or consultant and client department. |
| Client-consultant conflict | Conflict between the consulting team and client representatives over advice, scope, timing, evidence, or responsibilities. |

Common causes:

- work overload;
- deadlines;
- fear of job loss or blame;
- unclear communication;
- lack of trust;
- loss of control;
- poor timing;
- weak sponsorship;
- scope ambiguity;
- stakeholder politics.

Conflict response:

1. Separate person, behaviour, issue, and decision.
2. Prepare purpose, evidence, desired outcome, and likely objections.
3. Use neutral language and observable facts.
4. Listen for interests underneath positions.
5. Explain trade-offs and implications.
6. Escalate through governance if authority limits are reached.

Exam sentence:

> A consultant should treat difficult behaviour as information first, diagnose the cause of conflict, respond with evidence and neutral language, and escalate through governance when the issue threatens delivery or exceeds authority.

Common mistake: defining conflict only as the client clashing with the consultant's recommendation.

### 10. Close-Out

Close-out confirms whether the engagement has met scope, what remains unresolved, and what the client must do next.

Close-out can involve:

- final presentation;
- leader review;
- legal, risk, or quality assurance checks;
- final iterations;
- user acceptance;
- sponsor sign-off;
- handover;
- support arrangements;
- unresolved issue and residual risk documentation;
- lessons learned;
- project board or governance reporting.

Close-out report types:

- analytical report;
- lessons learned report;
- follow-on action recommendations;
- ROI report;
- end-of-project report.

Exam sentence:

> Close-out protects the client and consultant by confirming acceptance, documenting unresolved issues and residual risks, transferring ownership, preserving lessons learned, and creating a formal point for sign-off and future work.

Common mistake: saying close-out is just "providing the solution". It is a formal completion and transition process.

## High-Value Distinctions

### Executive Summary Versus Introduction

- Executive summary: decision-focused summary of the main offer, recommendation, or conclusion.
- Introduction: background, scope, document structure, and context.

### Scope Change Versus Informal Help

- Scope change should be assessed for cost, time, resourcing, quality, and deliverables.
- Once agreed, it should be documented in the client agreement or change-control process.
- Informal "yes" answers create scope creep and later conflict.

### Trust Components

Trust is built through:

- credibility: believable expertise and words;
- reliability: doing what was promised;
- intimacy: client feels safe sharing sensitive issues;
- low self-orientation: client believes the consultant is focused on the client's interest, not ego or sale.

### Hypothesis-Driven Consulting

Use this when a problem is ambiguous and analysis needs focus.

- Start with a provisional, testable explanation or answer.
- Define what evidence would support or reject it.
- Gather evidence through data, interviews, observation, surveys, or system analysis.
- Update the hypothesis when evidence changes.
- Do not defend the first idea blindly.

Exam sentence:

> A hypothesis is a testable working answer that focuses learning; it should guide evidence collection, not become a fixed conclusion.

### Report Writing

A consulting report should answer the client's question, not record a diary of activity.

Common structure:

- table of contents;
- executive summary;
- introduction with key question, aim, context, and scope;
- approach;
- answers to key questions;
- findings and recommendations;
- roadmap or implementation direction;
- appendices.

Common report mistakes:

- writing a diary of work completed;
- patronising the client;
- delivering by weight rather than relevance;
- failing to re-check the brief;
- introducing new material at the end;
- using overly complex diagrams;
- giving every thought instead of the reasoning the client needs.

## Common 10-Mark Answer Skeletons

### Problem-Solving Question

1. State whether the problem is structured, semi-structured, or unstructured.
2. Prove the label using scenario facts.
3. Name the IESE six-step process.
4. Apply each step to the case.
5. Explain trade-offs, stakeholders, risks, and action plan.

### Proposal Question

1. Identify the request type if relevant: RFI, EOI, RFQ, RFT, or RFP.
2. Explain proposal purpose.
3. Use proposal sections.
4. Apply each section to the scenario.
5. Discuss risks: assumptions, scope, pricing, timeline, capability, governance.

### Engagement Life-Cycle Question

1. List the five stages.
2. Explain what happens in each.
3. Apply each stage to the scenario.
4. Identify one risk each stage controls.
5. Connect early decisions to delivery and close-out.

### Meetings, Interviews, And Stakeholders Question

1. Identify what evidence or decision is needed.
2. Choose meeting, interview, workshop, or mix.
3. Select stakeholders and justify why.
4. State format and question style.
5. Explain how evidence will be recorded and followed up.

### Risk And Governance Question

1. Define risk and governance.
2. Identify risks from scenario facts.
3. Explain consequence for client, consultant, delivery, or relationship.
4. Propose mitigation.
5. Assign owner or escalation path.

### Conflict Question

1. Classify conflict: intragroup, intergroup, or client-consultant.
2. Prove the label using facts.
3. Identify causes.
4. Propose a neutral, evidence-based response.
5. Escalate through governance if needed.

### Close-Out Question

1. State what close-out is confirming.
2. Check deliverables against scope.
3. Include user acceptance and sponsor sign-off.
4. Document unresolved issues and residual risks.
5. Explain handover, support, lessons learned, and relationship preservation.

## Phrases That Lift Marks

- "This matters because..."
- "The scenario evidence is..."
- "The consultant should not jump straight to a solution because..."
- "This creates a delivery risk because..."
- "The governance response should be..."
- "The recommendation is..."
- "This should be documented as an assumption/exclusion/change request."
- "The client owns the decision, but the consultant should make the trade-off explicit."
- "The issue should be escalated when it exceeds the consultant's authority."

## Avoid These Exam Mistakes

- Listing framework steps without applying them to the case.
- Using vague words like "fix", "help", "deal", or "solution" without explaining the consulting implication.
- Treating the client's stated symptom as the real problem.
- Forgetting stakeholders and trust.
- Ignoring assumptions, exclusions, scope, and governance.
- Giving recommendations without evidence.
- Saying yes to extra client requests without discussing cost, timeline, quality, and scope.
- Ending close-out at "deliver the solution" without sign-off, handover, residual risks, and lessons learned.
