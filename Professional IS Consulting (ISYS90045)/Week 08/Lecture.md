## Key Concepts

### Meetings in Consulting

- Meetings are used to govern the engagement, make decisions, manage risk, align stakeholders, and maintain momentum.
- A useful consulting meeting should have a clear purpose, relevant participants, prepared materials, time discipline, and visible actions.
- Meeting quality affects trust. Poorly run meetings waste client time and signal weak control of the engagement.
- The consultant's role may shift between presenter, facilitator, analyst, note-taker, challenger, and advisor.
- The Week 8 slides distinguish meetings, interviews and workshops as different information-gathering tools rather than interchangeable communication formats.
- Meetings are usually small-group, agenda-driven, conversation-based sessions with moderate breadth and lower depth than interviews or workshops.
- Meetings should be called for a specific purpose such as information gathering, understanding, evaluation, design, problem definition, or analysis.
- Effective meetings require cultural expectations from the beginning, including promptness, one speaker at a time, no private side conversations, and participation during the meeting rather than afterwards.
- A meeting should not be the default response to every coordination need. If the issue can be resolved by email, chat, or a short call, a full meeting may waste client and consultant time.
- Poor meetings often fail because they have too many people, dominant personalities, off-topic discussion, low attention, technical friction in online/hybrid settings, or no decision at the end.
- The lecturer framed time as a scarce professional resource: accepting or calling vague meetings without purpose is equivalent to taking other people's working time without justification.
- The agenda should focus participants on accomplishment. The OPQRST agenda technique covers order/objectives, process, starting question, recording method, supplies/tools, and timing.
- Meeting preparation should include a participant list, key decision makers, logistics and note-taking roles, a realistic duration, and a commitment to start and end on time.
- Facilitators need to keep discussion on target while ensuring participants are heard. They should use data rather than personal criticism, analyse where things can go wrong before jumping to solutions, and seek consensus before resorting to voting.
- Meeting follow-up matters: minutes or agreed actions should be sent soon after the meeting, ideally within 48 hours.

### Interviewing in Consulting

- Interviews help consultants gather evidence, understand context, test assumptions, map stakeholder perspectives, and identify hidden issues.
- Good interviews use a mix of open, closed, and probing questions.
- Interviewers should create enough safety for honest answers while maintaining professional independence.
- Interview notes should distinguish between direct observations, stakeholder opinions, consultant interpretations, and follow-up evidence needs.
- Interviews are usually one-to-one, structured around a small number of points, and provide more depth but less breadth than meetings or workshops.
- Because the information flow is often one-way, the consultant must prepare questions carefully and listen for what is said, omitted, or assumed.
- Interviews are conversations with a purpose. The slides describe them as planned, directed, and controlled rather than casual discussion.
- Before interviewing, consultants should confirm the purpose, whether another method would be more efficient, whether the format is individual or group/panel, who will ask questions, who will take notes, and what setting is appropriate.
- Interview format should match the evidence needed. Face-to-face and video formats provide richer social cues, telephone can support lower-cost or more anonymous conversations, and email can work when time zones or asynchronous work make live interviews impractical.
- Anonymity can be crucial when interviewees may face workplace consequences for honest answers, such as discussing underpayment, process failure, or politically sensitive organisational problems.
- Question design shapes evidence quality. Open questions support narrative and explanation; closed or numeric questions are better when the consultant needs specific facts for analysis.
- Active listening includes playing back the answer, confirming understanding, giving the interviewee space to correct the interpretation, and avoiding a rigid script when follow-up questions are needed.
- Notes provide an evidence trail for later recommendations. If a business decision relies on interviews, the consultant needs a defensible record of what was asked, said, and inferred.

### Consulting Interview Technique

- The lecture connected information-gathering interviews with consulting job interviews because both test structured thinking, question design, listening, and concise recommendation.
- In a consulting-style case interview, the interviewer is often less interested in the final answer than in how the candidate structures the problem, asks clarifying questions, identifies required data, and communicates assumptions.
- Strong consulting questions are targeted to the client's situation, such as customer segments, regions, offerings, competitor pricing, and changes in behaviour. Weak questions stay vague or jump directly to generic tactics.
- Recommendations should be specific to the client context. For example, a targeted happy-hour strategy for a pub chain shows more sector reasoning than a generic recommendation to "do more marketing."
- Consulting interview structures commonly combine a short introduction, a case or problem-solving task, a market-sizing or estimation task, behavioural and fit questions, and a final wrap-up.
- Market-sizing questions test structured assumptions rather than exact knowledge. A defensible estimate breaks the problem into variables such as population, relevant segment, usage frequency, and price or volume assumptions.
- Behavioural questions test fit for consulting work, including influencing without authority, working with difficult stakeholders, and managing competing priorities under deadline pressure.

### Facilitation and Stakeholder Engagement

- Meetings and interviews are not neutral information channels. The way questions are asked shapes what people reveal.
- Consultants should be alert to power dynamics, silence, disagreement, defensiveness, and overly dominant voices.
- Workshops can create shared understanding where individual interviews reveal fragmented views.
- Outputs from meetings and interviews should be written up promptly and checked where appropriate.

### Reading Notes

- Weir, Becker, and Blair study workshops as a way to engage product managers with software security through developer participation.
- Their intervention used workshops to help teams identify threats, assess likelihood and impact, and express security improvements in terms meaningful to product managers.
- The study found workshops could improve threat assessment and product-management engagement, especially where teams had less security expertise.
- For consulting, the key lesson is that workshops can translate specialist concerns into business-relevant language and create shared prioritisation.
- The paper also shows a limitation: lightweight workshops rely on participant knowledge, so facilitators must know when extra evidence or expertise is needed.
- The study involved 88 developers across eight organisations and found improved product-management engagement in most groups.
- The workshops were especially useful for teams with lower security maturity and when facilitated by line managers.
- Developers could identify security selling points such as security consultancy, customer choice, robustness, competitive differentiation, implied requirements, and avoiding disaster.
- Blockers included poor communication, multiple stakeholders, expectations of "security for free", unknown cost or impact, limited product manager time, denial, and practical constraints.
- Motivators included friendly customers, external policies, principled insistence, stakeholder value, better communication, logging decisions, and structured workshops.
- For consulting communication, the paper shows that specialist concerns become more actionable when reframed as business value, customer choice, risk reduction, or product differentiation.

## Examples

- A weekly steering meeting should focus on decisions, risks, issues, milestones, and unresolved dependencies rather than a long activity diary.
- An interview with a process owner may reveal informal workarounds that are invisible in formal documentation.
- A workshop can use prioritisation methods, such as impact-likelihood mapping, to turn many concerns into a manageable action list.

## Summary

Meetings and interviews are core consulting communication tools. They gather evidence, shape relationships, surface assumptions, and support decisions. The reading extends this by showing how workshops can convert specialist knowledge into shared priorities and business-facing action.
