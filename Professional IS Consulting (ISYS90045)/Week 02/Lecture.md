## Key Concepts

### What a Proposal Does

- A consulting proposal is a written offer from a potential supplier to a client.
- It normally explains the client's problem, the services offered, the proposed approach, the budget, the team, assumptions, and evidence of similar work.
- Proposals can be formal or informal. Formal proposals are common in public-sector or high-value procurement; informal proposals may follow from existing relationships and move quickly into an engagement letter or contract.
- Proposal writing can be a significant workstream because bids require sales knowledge, methodology design, technical and industry input, pricing, and careful review.
- A proposal has to satisfy both sides: the client wants value, confidence, and manageable risk; the consultant needs a deliverable scope, price, and timeline that can be delivered profitably.
- Proposal formats can include written documents, PDFs, slide decks, videos, messaging, or informal exchanges, but the final agreement still needs to be documented in a contract.

### Proposal Request Types

- Request for information (RFI): used to understand what products or services are available in the market.
- Expression of interest (EOI): used to test supplier interest before a later proposal process.
- Request for quote (RFQ): focused on pricing, often for standardised or commoditised services.
- Request for tender (RFT): formal request for suppliers to submit bids, common in government procurement.
- Request for proposal (RFP): similar to an RFT but more common in private-sector contexts.

### Basic Proposal Structure

- Executive summary: summarises the main points and should not merely introduce the document.
- Introduction: gives background, scope, proposal structure, and why the consultant is suited to the work.
- Understanding of needs: shows the consultant has interpreted the real issue rather than repeating the RFP wording.
- Engagement methodology and timelines: explains how the work will be done, governed, sequenced, and delivered.
- Pricing and assumptions: states cost, budget logic, exclusions, discounts, and limits such as the number of workshops or analysis depth.
- Team CVs: identifies who will actually do the work and what expertise they bring.
- Similar work: demonstrates relevant experience through concise evidence and outcomes.
- The executive summary is closer to an abstract than a background section: it should tell a time-poor decision maker what the proposal recommends or offers.
- Pricing reveals the work implied by the proposal. Missing items such as testing, training, workshops, support, or licensing can signal that the proposal is incomplete.
- "Understanding of needs" is where the consultant shows interpretation. Simply restating the client's RFP text does not prove insight.

### Understanding the Audience

- Proposal content should be shaped by the client's decision makers, technical evaluators, procurement staff, and impacted stakeholders.
- Senior decision makers may read for risk, value, credibility, and strategic fit. Functional staff may check technical detail and mandatory criteria.
- Technical detail should be proportionate. Some clients expect significant technical evidence; others need the implications translated into business language.
- Before submission, consultants should seek opportunities to clarify client needs and test assumptions, while respecting procurement rules around equal access for bidders.
- The transcript used the email migration example to show that consultants must avoid selling generic offerings. If the client wants email migration, an unrelated platform pitch signals poor audience understanding.
- In formal procurement, every question and answer may be shared with all bidders, so clarification has to follow the client's rules.
- Proposal detail can be evaluated as a checklist before senior decision makers ever see it. Missing a small required item can remove the bid before its substantive value is considered.

### Bid or No-Bid Judgement

- Before bidding, consultants should ask whether the work fits the firm's capabilities, risk appetite, relationship position, and commercial priorities.
- A bid can take substantial effort, so the firm should consider whether it knows the client, understands the real work, can win, and can deliver safely.
- Mandatory criteria matter. Proposals can be eliminated for missing required sections, certificates, qualifications, references, or pricing constraints.
- Large wins often happen in stages: initial proposal, shortlisting, presentations, further submissions, and contract negotiation.
- A new boutique consultancy may choose not to bid if the tender requires a long track record of similar projects it cannot prove.
- The lowest price is not automatically best for the consultant or the client. A price that cannot cover effort, team cost, quality, and risk creates delivery failure later.
- Bid visibility inside the firm matters so legal, compliance, business development, lead partners, and resource managers know what is being promised.
- Some firms will not bid without an existing client relationship because the probability of winning may not justify the proposal effort.

### Reading Notes

- Sobel's client relationship matrix distinguishes between narrow transactional relationships and broader trusted partnerships.
- The goal is to move beyond "expert for hire" by becoming a trusted advisor and, at the firm level, a trusted partner with many-to-many relationships.
- Strong relationship management requires relationship capital, agenda-setting, institutionalising the relationship, cross-firm collaboration, and a pipeline of people who can manage major client relationships.
- For proposal work, the reading reinforces that winning work is not just a document exercise. It depends on the relationship position the consultant has before the RFP appears.
- Sobel argues that competition, commoditisation, client sophistication and transparent markets make services harder to differentiate on service features alone, so enduring relationships become a major source of risk reduction and value.
- "Expert for hire" relationships are usually narrow and reactive. "Vendor" relationships may be broad but still exposed to RFP competition if they lack trusted-advisor status.
- A trusted partner both shapes the client's agenda and mobilises the firm's wider capabilities through many-to-many relationships, not just one strong individual contact.
- Agenda-setting means understanding and helping improve the client's critical priorities rather than waiting passively for requests.

## Examples

- A proposal for an email migration should not only list technical steps. It should show understanding of business disruption, training, data migration risk, user adoption, and success measures.
- A government RFT may require strict compliance with submission instructions, insurance evidence, staff references, and mandatory response templates.
- A private client with an existing relationship may accept a shorter proposal if budget, scope, method, and responsibility are clear.
- An RFP may require URLs for previous work, prohibit URLs because printed submissions must be static, or say nothing. The consultant must read the instructions rather than assume.
- A proposal may need to disclose lost clients, terminated contracts, insurance thresholds, or second-round presentation requirements.

## Summary

Proposal writing combines problem interpretation, audience awareness, risk judgement, sales strategy, and disciplined structure. A strong proposal shows that the consultant understands the client's real need, can deliver credibly, and has thought through value, risk, method, people, and assumptions.
