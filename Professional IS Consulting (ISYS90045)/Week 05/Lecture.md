## Topic

Giving Professional Advice; AT1 Presentations

## Readings

- Maister, D. H. (2005). *Advice to a young professional*. DavidMaister.com. Available on LMS.

## Key Concepts

### Giving Professional Advice

- Professional advice is a structured response to a client's problem, not simply an opinion.
- Good advice links the client's objective, relevant evidence, analysis, options, trade-offs, and a recommended path.
- Advice should be clear about assumptions, scope, limits, risks, and dependencies.
- In consulting, advice often needs to be both analytically sound and organisationally usable.

### Advice Versus Information

- Information describes facts, observations, or data.
- Analysis explains what the information means.
- Advice recommends what the client should do, based on analysis and judgement.
- A common consulting weakness is providing lots of information without making the judgement explicit.

### Advice Quality

- Useful advice is specific enough to guide action.
- Advice should be proportionate to the client's authority, resources, time, appetite for risk, and organisational constraints.
- Professional advice does not hide trade-offs. It should make consequences visible.
- Where evidence is incomplete, the consultant should explain confidence level and what additional evidence would change the recommendation.

### Advice Delivery

- Advice should be sequenced so the client can follow the logic from problem to recommendation.
- The consultant should anticipate objections and address them respectfully.
- Advice may need to be delivered through conversation, presentation, report, workshop, or executive briefing.
- Because advice can challenge existing plans or stakeholder interests, delivery requires tact as well as clarity.

### Reading Notes

- Maister's advice reading reframes professional success as more than intelligence, qualifications, or brand-name employment. Energy, initiative, discipline, courage, relationship skill, and character matter.
- Logic and technical rationality only take a consultant so far; influence depends on understanding how to work with other people.
- Character is central in business relationships because clients, colleagues, managers and suppliers need to believe the consultant will act responsibly.
- Broken promises are remembered more than kept promises, so consultants should avoid accepting work they cannot deliver and should repair damaged trust when mistakes happen.
- Business development is a professional skill to practise early. Strong relationship skills create more choice about who to work for and what work to accept.
- Criticising people rarely persuades them. The goal is not to prove the consultant is right; it is to help move the other person toward a useful decision.

## Examples

- Weak advice: "Improve communication." Stronger advice: "Introduce a weekly sponsor update with decisions, risks, and next actions because current delays are caused by unresolved cross-team dependencies."
- Weak advice: "Buy a new system." Stronger advice: "Do not select a system until process ownership and data quality problems are resolved; otherwise the new system will reproduce the same failures."
- A consultant may recommend a staged pilot when evidence supports the direction but uncertainty remains about adoption, cost, or operational impact.

## Questions

- What separates professional advice from a personal opinion?
- How should advice change when evidence is incomplete?
- How can consultants make trade-offs visible without overwhelming the client?
- What makes advice actionable?
- Why might relationship skill and character matter as much as technical intelligence in professional advice?

## Summary

Giving professional advice requires judgement, structure, evidence, and client awareness. The consultant's role is to help the client make a better decision by clarifying the problem, explaining the reasoning, and recommending action with appropriate confidence and caveats.
