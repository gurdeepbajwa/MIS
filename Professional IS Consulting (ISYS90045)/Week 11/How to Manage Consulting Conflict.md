# How to Manage Consulting Conflict

Course: [[Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)/Course Overview|Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)]]
Week: 11
Source: [[Professional IS Consulting (ISYS90045)/Week 11/Lecture|Week 11 Lecture]]
Diataxis type: How-to guide

## Purpose

Use this guide when a consulting scenario involves resistance, difficult stakeholders, poor cooperation, or conflict between teams.

## Steps

1. Identify the conflict type.
   - Intragroup conflict occurs within the same team.
   - Intergroup conflict occurs between groups, such as a client department and the consulting team.
2. Separate the person from the issue.
   - Treat difficult behaviour as evidence about fear, incentives, workload, trust, politics, or misunderstanding.
3. Clarify the interests behind the positions.
   - Ask what each party wants, what they fear, and what they need to keep doing their work.
4. Analyse before solving.
   - Do not jump straight to a technical fix or persuasion attempt before understanding the cause.
5. Choose a path forward.
   - Negotiate timing, scope, training, communication, or stakeholder involvement.
   - Bring in a senior consultant, mentor, or better-suited colleague if the conflict exceeds your authority or skill fit.
6. Manage emotions.
   - Call a timeout, reset the meeting, change the setting, or return when the facts and expectations are clearer.
7. Document the outcome.
   - Record decisions, assumptions, unresolved risks, and any escalation path.

## Useful Moves

| Situation | Move |
| --- | --- |
| Stakeholders think the consultant is there to cut jobs | Clarify the engagement purpose, what will change, and what will not change. |
| The stakeholder is overloaded | Negotiate timing or reduce unnecessary meeting burden. |
| The consultant is the wrong fit for the conversation | Bring in a colleague with stronger elicitation or relationship skills. |
| Negotiation reaches an authority limit | Escalate to a senior person or governance forum. |
| Emotions rise | Pause, restate the issue neutrally, and return to the facts and interests. |

## Exam Use

A strong exam answer should:

- name the type of conflict;
- use case evidence to justify that label;
- identify likely causes;
- propose a response that fits the consultant's authority;
- explain how the response reduces conflict without ignoring the business problem.

## Common Mistakes

- Labelling someone as difficult without explaining the underlying cause.
- Treating resistance as personal hostility.
- Trying to persuade harder instead of gaining perspective.
- Offering a solution before analysing the conflict.
- Extending scope just to make the conflict go away.
