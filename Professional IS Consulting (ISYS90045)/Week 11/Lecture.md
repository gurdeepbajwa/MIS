## Course-Sourced Material

### Dealing with Problems and Conflict

- Consulting engagements create conflict because they involve change, uncertainty, competing priorities, limited budgets, and stakeholder exposure.
- Problems may arise from scope ambiguity, late information, unrealistic expectations, weak sponsorship, poor stakeholder alignment, or delivery constraints.
- Difficult behaviour should be treated as information before it is treated as obstruction. It may reveal fear, incentives, politics, lack of trust, or genuine flaws in the work.
- Consultants should separate the person, the behaviour, the issue, and the decision required.
- Conflict involves friction or disagreement where the beliefs or actions of one person or group are resisted by another.
- Intragroup conflict occurs inside the same group or team.
- Intergroup conflict occurs between groups, such as a consulting team and a client department.
- A difficult stakeholder may be reacting to the consultant as a messenger or perceived enforcer rather than to the consultant personally.
- The lecture example used an accounts department resisting consultants who were changing an information system. The resistance could reflect fear of job loss, workload pressure, distrust, lack of information, or frustration at being told how to work.

### Managing Difficult Conversations

- Prepare the purpose, evidence, desired outcome, and likely objections before the conversation.
- Use neutral language that describes observable facts and impact.
- Listen for interests underneath positions.
- Name trade-offs clearly and avoid personalising disagreement.
- Escalate through governance when the issue exceeds the consultant's authority or threatens delivery.
- Use negotiation principles from earlier weeks: do not assume a fixed-position split is the best answer. Look for the underlying interests.
- Gain perspective by asking how important the problem is, what happens if it is not solved, and whether another consultant or senior person should help.
- Help others gain perspective by clarifying what the consulting team is and is not trying to do.
- Use analysis before solution: clarify the cause of conflict before pushing a fix.
- Focus on the path forward rather than on blame.
- Manage emotions by calling a timeout, returning to the issue when the environment is calmer, or changing the personnel, timing, or setting.
- Avoid overfunctioning. Doing more and more to persuade the client can look controlling or desperate, and it may create scope creep.
- Remain self-aware. A consultant's personality, communication style, or technical focus may fit some stakeholder conversations better than others.
- If negotiation reaches the consultant's authority limit, involve a senior person or recognise that the firm may need to make a hard choice.

### Causes of Conflict

- Work overload or time pressure.
- Deadline clashes, such as end-of-financial-year pressure for an accounts team.
- Lack of knowledge about what the consulting work will change.
- Fear of job loss, blame, or loss of control.
- Boredom or frustration from long meetings that do not respect stakeholder time.
- Personal pressures outside work.
- Poor consultant fit for the conversation, such as sending a highly technical consultant into a sensitive requirements-elicitation situation without support.

### Closing Out the Engagement

- Close-out confirms whether the work has met scope, what remains unresolved, and what the client needs to do next.
- Close-out can involve final presentations, leadership review, quality assurance, legal or risk checks, final iterations, sign-off, handover, and lessons learned.
- A good close-out protects the relationship by making outcomes, limitations, ownership, and next steps explicit.
- The end of an engagement is also a relationship-management moment. It affects repeat work, referrals, reputation, and whether the client acts on the advice.
- Close-out belongs at the end of the engagement life cycle after risk assessment, winning the work, contractual negotiation, delivery, and project management.
- Formal sign-off creates a fixed point of completion and evidence that products, deliverables, or outcomes were received and accepted.
- Sign-off matters for time and cost. Once the client has accepted the deliverable, extra features or changes should be handled through a new agreement or maintenance arrangement, not assumed as free work.
- Sign-off should not only be internal to the consulting firm. Client acceptance and, where relevant, user acceptance should be documented.
- Formal closure can also document partial failure or unresolved objectives. This protects both parties by making the remaining work explicit.

### Close-Out Objectives and Activities

- Verify user acceptance, not just sponsor approval.
- Ensure the product, report, system, or recommendation can be supported after the project team disbands.
- Review project performance against baseline documents, expected benefits, costs, schedule, scope, and quality expectations.
- Assess benefits and return on investment where possible.
- Address open issues, risks, and unresolved work.
- Prepare for expected closure as the project end date approaches.
- Prepare for unexpected closure if new management, project governance, or delivery conditions require the project to stop early.
- Update issue registers, review status reports, and record what has been delivered.
- Complete handover of information, licences, product ownership, responsibility, and support arrangements.
- Disband the project team and prevent further charging to the closed project account.
- Provide closure information to the project board or governance body.
- Use legal, quality assurance, or risk review where the deliverable is sensitive, formal, or contractually exposed.

### Close-Out Report Types

- Analytical report: explains success, failure, and what could have gone better.
- Lessons learned report: records learning for future engagements, including internal learning for the consulting firm.
- Follow-on action recommendations: gives next steps to maintenance, customisation, training, implementation, or support teams.
- ROI report: assesses whether expected benefits or financial outcomes are being realised.
- End-of-project report: summarises project performance, completion status, open issues, and closure evidence.
- Failure-related records are also valuable because legal issues, relationship breakdown, payment disputes, reputational damage, or underused products can become learning material for future work.

### Personal Fit for Consulting

- Consulting can offer rapid learning, variety, problem solving, exposure to leaders, and career acceleration.
- It can also involve intensity, ambiguity, travel, long hours, emotional labour, and pressure to sell or deliver under constraint.
- A sustainable consulting career requires self-awareness about preferred work style, stress tolerance, learning goals, and long-term aspirations.

## Examples

- A sponsor wants a finding softened before final presentation. The consultant should discuss evidence, wording, consequences, and professional obligations rather than simply comply or refuse.
- A stakeholder who resists an interview may be concerned about blame, workload, or loss of control.
- A close-out meeting should confirm decisions, residual risks, ownership of actions, and how unresolved issues will be handled.
- An accounts team may resist a system-change consultant because they fear automation, job loss, extra work during a busy period, or being judged for current processes.
- If a client asks for extra post-sign-off changes, the consultant should check scope and contract terms before agreeing. The work may require a new maintenance or change-control agreement.

## Summary

Consulting problems are often relational as well as technical. Effective consultants manage conflict with evidence, neutrality, listening, and governance. Closing out well ensures that the client understands the advice, accepts ownership of next steps, and retains trust in the consultant.
