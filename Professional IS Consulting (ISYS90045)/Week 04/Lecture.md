## Readings

## Key Concepts

### Professional Communication

- Consulting communication must be audience-aware, structured, concise, and evidence-based.
- Professional communication is not only presentation polish. It includes listening, framing, tone, sequencing, confidence, responsiveness, and the ability to explain uncertainty.
- The same content should be adapted for different audiences: executive sponsors, operational staff, technical specialists, procurement teams, and affected users.
- Consultants need to distinguish between information, analysis, recommendation, and persuasion.
- The transcript repeatedly framed communication as translating technical material for non-technical stakeholders without treating the audience as incapable.
- Use the client's language and terminology where possible. This signals that the consultant understands the organisation rather than presenting a generic technical explanation.
- Presentation mode matters: in-person, online, and hybrid presentations create different demands for engagement, technology setup, question handling, and audience awareness.

### Presenting Consulting Work

- A consulting presentation should make the central message clear early, then support it with evidence, reasoning, and implications.
- Strong presentations usually answer: What is the problem? What did we find? Why does it matter? What should be done? What risks or trade-offs remain?
- The presenter should manage both content and relationship: credibility, pacing, questions, disagreement, and stakeholder reactions.
- Visual material should reduce complexity, not decorate it. Charts, tables, and diagrams should serve a specific message.
- Presentations need a clear beginning, middle, and end. The audience should know the main point, follow the supporting logic, and leave with the intended action or understanding.
- A short presentation should have one clear message. It is better to distil the core idea than to compress too much technical detail.
- Slides and videos should support the speaker, not replace them. If the audience watches a video, that is time they are not assessing or connecting with the presenter.
- Presenters need backup plans for failed slides, internet, QR codes, demos, or audio. The consultant should still be able to continue the message.
- Speaking is more engaging than reading. Notes should be prompts, not a script.

### Trust and Advice

- Trust is central because clients often act on advice that involves risk, cost, uncertainty, and organisational consequences.
- The Trusted Advisor reading frames trust through credibility, reliability, intimacy, and self-orientation.
- Credibility is about whether the consultant's words and expertise are believable.
- Reliability is about whether the consultant does what they said they would do.
- Intimacy is about whether the client feels safe discussing sensitive issues.
- Low self-orientation means the consultant appears focused on the client's interests rather than their own ego, sale, or preferred solution.

### Communication Risks

- Overstating certainty can damage credibility if later evidence changes.
- Using too much technical language can make advice hard to evaluate for non-specialists.
- Avoiding difficult messages may preserve short-term comfort but weaken the advisory role.
- Presenting recommendations without showing the reasoning can make advice feel arbitrary or sales-driven.
- Common delivery mistakes include following a script despite audience feedback, using text-heavy slides, facing the screen instead of the audience, hiding behind the lectern, and failing to adapt when time is short.
- If questions are allowed during the presentation, they can improve engagement but may disrupt flow. The presenter should decide and signal question handling deliberately.
- Body language, eye contact, pace, pauses, gestures, and movement all affect whether the audience experiences the consultant as credible and engaged.

### Reading Notes

- Maister, Galford, and Green position trust as the foundation of advisory relationships.
- The reading is useful for presentation work because presentations are moments where clients judge credibility, reliability, care, and motive.
- A consultant can improve trust by being clear about what is known, what is uncertain, what has been assumed, and what is recommended.
- The trusted advisor role requires listening before solving, admitting limits, and reducing self-orientation in client interactions.
- Maister's trust reading emphasises that trust is not automatic in professional services; it must be earned through behaviour that gives the other person evidence they can rely on you.
- Low trust inside a professional firm reduces collaboration, knowledge sharing, client handover, mentoring, motivation, retention, efficiency and quality.
- Clients often cannot fully verify a professional's technical work, so they look for signs that the advisor will put the client's interests ahead of their own short-term revenue.
- Trust is built when actions match words, values are visible, and the advisor shows a long-term commitment to the relationship.

## Examples

- For executives, a presentation might emphasise decision options, financial and operational implications, risk, and recommended action.
- For technical staff, the same presentation may need method detail, assumptions, architecture, constraints, and implementation implications.
- Saying "we do not yet have enough evidence to recommend that option" can build more trust than pretending to know.
- A charity shop audience may need plain explanations of business value and risk, while a room of system administrators may expect technical terminology and implementation detail.
- If a live demo fails, the consultant can switch to screenshots, printed slides, a short verbal walkthrough, or a teammate-managed fix while maintaining audience engagement.

## Questions

- How does communication change when presenting to executives versus technical staff?
- What is the difference between sounding confident and overstating certainty?
- How can a consultant show low self-orientation in a presentation?
- What makes a recommendation persuasive without becoming manipulative?
- What should a consultant do when presentation technology fails?
- What behaviours give a client evidence that a consultant can be trusted?

## Summary

Professional communication turns analysis into usable advice. The week connects presentation skill with trust: clients are more likely to act when advice is clear, evidence-based, appropriately tailored, and delivered by someone whose motive appears client-focused.
