## Key Concepts

### Consulting as a Professional Role

- Consulting work is usually framed around helping organisations solve problems they cannot, or do not want to, solve alone.
- Consultants may be hired for specialist expertise, external perspective, temporary capacity, credibility with stakeholders, or structured delivery methods.
- In IS consulting, the work is not limited to technical advice. It can include strategy, business analysis, architecture, change, project delivery, testing, governance, and stakeholder communication.
- The consultant's asset base is not only technical knowledge. Reputation and professional networks are central because clients buy trust before they buy advice.
- The transcript emphasised that consulting is a commercial arrangement: the client is paying for expertise, judgement, and usable outcomes, not just effort.
- Clients often engage consultants because they lack time, internal resources, specialist knowledge, or confidence that an internal view is sufficiently independent.

### Types of Consulting Firms

- Tier 1 or large management consultancies tend to offer global reach, established methods, recognised intellectual property, and premium-priced delivery.
- Vendor consultancies are connected to products, platforms, or implementation ecosystems, such as software, hardware, cloud, systems integration, or analyst services.
- Independent consultancies are less tied to a product and may focus on project management, strategy, architecture, change management, or business analysis.
- Niche consulting firms are built around focused competence, often from senior experts with deep industry, product, functional, jurisdictional, or technical knowledge.
- Internal consultants sit inside an organisation, often in strategic functions, PMOs, business analysis groups, or specialist advisory roles. They may know the organisation better than external consultants but can have less independence.
- Partnership firms have direct profit, loss, and risk accountability because partners own the firm and are exposed when a project fails.
- Vendor consultants may bring deep product expertise, but their advice can be shaped by the product they sell. Clients should understand this potential conflict.
- Analyst firms influence technology decisions by producing market assessments, reports, and frameworks that CIOs may use to validate recommendations.
- AI may change the consulting career pyramid by automating some junior research and drafting tasks, increasing the importance of judgement, synthesis, and client-facing capability.

### Consulting Skills

- Subject matter expertise is necessary but not sufficient. Consultants also need influence, trust-building, problem solving, management skills, social skills, and personal resilience.
- Consulting involves ambiguity. The client may not have defined the real problem clearly, and the consultant must often diagnose before recommending.
- Typical consulting delivery includes interviews, workshops, analysis, reporting, presentations, project management, relationship management, and sales support.
- As careers progress, the emphasis often shifts from doing delivery work to managing projects, selling work, maintaining relationships, and building the firm.
- Continuous professional development matters because professional knowledge dates quickly. A consultant whose knowledge is not refreshed becomes less credible and less useful.
- Consultants need to work autonomously with incomplete information, then form defensible recommendations based on evidence and professional judgement.

### Professional Conduct

- Clients notice reliability, listening, punctuality, manners, grooming, tone, and whether the consultant appears genuinely interested in the client's context.
- Professionalism is practical: it shapes whether the client feels safe sharing information and whether they trust the consultant's judgement.
- Common consulting trade-offs include learning speed, variety, intensity, travel, career progression, stress, life balance, and long-term career goals.
- First impressions form quickly, so appearance, posture, eye contact, personal space, and punctuality affect whether the client is ready to trust the consultant.
- Professional appearance should match the client context. Overdressing or underdressing can both create distance if it makes the consultant look disconnected from the client's environment.
- Promptness is part of respect. Arriving rushed, late, or unprepared creates avoidable friction before the consulting work begins.

### Reading Notes

- The "day in the life" reading/video should be treated as a practical lens on the role: consulting is a mix of analysis, communication, meetings, client management, and structured problem solving rather than only giving expert opinions.
- The Reesan Group case should be used as an applied client context for practising consulting judgement. Keep the focus on client problem framing, stakeholders, evidence, and advice quality rather than case narrative details.

## Examples

- A client may think they need a new system, but the consulting question may actually involve process design, governance, data quality, stakeholder alignment, or capability gaps.
- A technically strong consultant can still fail if they cannot build trust, listen carefully, explain trade-offs, or adapt to the client's level of knowledge.
- An internal consultant may have strong organisational knowledge but must manage perceptions of independence and internal politics.
- A vendor consultant asked to recommend finance software may naturally recommend their own platform, so the client should interpret the advice with that commercial position in mind.
- A consultant entering a start-up client in formal corporate attire may look less aligned than one who matches the client's professional norms while still appearing credible.

## Questions

- What makes reputation and networks valuable consulting assets?
- How do external and internal consultants differ in credibility, independence, access, and influence?
- What parts of consulting appeal to you, and what parts create risk or stress?
- How might AI change entry-level consulting work, and which skills become more important as a result?

## Summary

Consulting is a professional advisory role built around problem solving, trust, communication, and delivery. IS consultants need technical credibility, but the lecture emphasises that influence, judgement, stakeholder management, and professional behaviour are what turn expertise into useful advice.
