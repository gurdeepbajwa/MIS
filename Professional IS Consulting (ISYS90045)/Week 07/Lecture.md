## Topic

Reesan Case Study; Professional Advice - Report Writing and Workshops

## Readings

- Garvin, D., & Margolis, J. (2015). *The art of giving and receiving advice*. Harvard Business Review. Available on LMS.

## Key Concepts

### Reesan Group Case Context

- Reesan is framed as a supermarket retail chain similar to familiar grocery retailers, making it a practical context for thinking about business operations, stores, inventory, logistics, systems, and margins.
- The case is used to practise understanding an organisational environment before giving advice.
- The board wants answers because growth ambitions, operational complexity, system issues, or declining maturity can create pressure to bring in consultants.
- The consulting task begins with understanding the business problem, not jumping straight to a technology solution.
- The case explains that Reesan grew by acquisition, which created architectural misalignment, duplicated practices, political tensions, and integration complexity.
- Jupiter was originally a source of efficiency, but regional specialisation, file-transfer coordination, large monolithic modules, and shared databases made change increasingly risky and expensive.
- Business and IT distrust became part of the problem: business leaders saw IT as reducing agility, while IT saw business as making impossible demands without consultation.
- External audits and consultants identified operational fragmentation and loss of process and information integrity as major contributors to Reesan's decline.
- The case's proposed direction is an enterprise architecture initiative that must be led from the top and involve both business and IT from the start.

### Professional Advice Through Reports

- Reports are one of the main tangible outputs a client retains after a consulting engagement.
- Reports can cover status, requirements, design, development, testing, gap analysis, feasibility, audit findings, advice, and implementation recommendations.
- A report should answer the client's problem, not merely document what the consultant did.
- The basic rule is to put yourself in the client's place: what do they need to know, how much detail do they need, and what are they paying for?
- A consulting report is usually a response to a client question or board concern, such as cost effectiveness, process delay, inventory problems, or growth constraints.
- Reports should communicate advantages and disadvantages honestly. They are not sales documents.
- Reports become a permanent record that can justify decisions after the consultant has left.
- Status reports during delivery help avoid surprises by surfacing progress, blockers, and issues early.

### Consulting Report Structure

- Table of contents.
- Executive summary, usually concise and decision-focused.
- Introduction with the key questions, context, aim, and scope.
- Approach explaining how the work was done.
- Answers to key questions, including reasons and alternatives where relevant.
- Findings and recommendations.
- Roadmap or implementation direction where useful.
- Appendices for interview notes, calculations, detailed evidence, and supporting material.
- Senior decision makers may only read the executive summary and cost sections, while technical or operational stakeholders may need the detailed evidence.
- The approach section should explain who was consulted, what documents were reviewed, what evidence was used, and how conclusions were reached.
- Appendices protect the reasoning: someone can later inspect the data, interview notes, calculations, or logic behind the recommendation.

### Report Writing Process

- First summarise verbally what you are trying to say in one sentence.
- Expand the summary, get feedback, and repeat until the core message is clear.
- List key points, organise them into a structure, and choose the best communication form for each point: graph, table, diagram, or prose.
- Write the report, rewrite the summary, let the report rest, then review and rewrite.
- Results and recommendations should connect to the scope, evidence, theory, experience, or framework used.

### Common Report Mistakes

- Writing a diary of activities instead of answering the client's question.
- Patronising the client or demonstrating knowledge for its own sake.
- Delivering by weight rather than relevance.
- Failing to re-check the brief.
- Introducing new material at the end.
- Using overly complex diagrams or tables.
- Sharing every piece of thinking instead of the reasoning the client needs.
- Poor grammar, spelling, or inappropriate content.

### Workshops

- Workshops are collaborative consulting tools for communication, requirements gathering, joint design, review, and problem solving.
- Facilitators need active listening, summarising, questioning, feedback, role-play, recording, writing up contributions, and troubleshooting skills.
- Workshop preparation should define aims, activity order, timing, group sizes, instructions, questions, and likely problems.
- Workshops are useful when the client needs shared understanding, not just information transfer.
- Workshops are usually run after engagement, during evidence gathering and delivery.
- A workshop should have an agenda, objective, activities, timing, participant plan, and expected outputs.
- A practical rule from the transcript was that a workshop may need several hours of preparation for each hour in the room.
- Workshop group design matters. Mixed-function groups can reveal dependencies and conflict, but separate workshops may be better when power dynamics prevent honest contribution.
- The facilitator should listen for what participants say directly and what their conversations reveal indirectly.
- Workshop evidence can reveal that the real problem is different from the original symptom described in the proposal.

### Reading Notes

- Garvin and Margolis treat giving and receiving advice as practical skills that can be learned rather than as passive wisdom exchange.
- The advice process should consider fit, shared understanding, option generation, decision convergence, and action.
- For consultants, this means advice should be dialogic: the client and advisor clarify the issue, test options, and maintain ownership of the decision.
- Advice should be treated as provisional where circumstances may change. Follow-up and learning matter.

## Examples

- A report for a feasibility study should make the recommendation and evidence clear before placing detailed calculations in appendices.
- A workshop can help stakeholders map a value chain when no single person understands the whole process.
- A report recommendation should acknowledge scope limits instead of implying that findings are universal.
- In Reesan, a workshop might map the value chain from selecting retail products through purchase, payment, inventory update, replenishment, and customer outcome.
- If a client says "the system is too slow", workshops may reveal that the true issue is resourcing, workflow handoff, duplicated forms, or mismatched data formats.

## Questions

- What does the client need from a consulting report after the consultant leaves?
- How is a report different from a record of work completed?
- When is a workshop better than a meeting or interview?
- How can advice remain client-owned rather than consultant-imposed?
- How should a consultant respond if workshops reveal a different problem from the one assumed in the proposal?
- How does the Reesan case show that technical debt can become an organisational trust problem?

## Summary

Professional advice is often delivered through reports and workshops. Reports turn evidence into structured recommendations; workshops create shared understanding and collaborative problem solving. Both require clarity about the client's question, scope, evidence, and decision needs.
