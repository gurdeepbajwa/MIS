## Key Concepts

### Course Integration

- The course can be understood as a full consulting arc: understand the role, assess the client and audience, propose work, negotiate the engagement, deliver advice, communicate findings, solve problems, manage conflict, and close out.
- The main recurring theme is trust. Trust affects whether clients select consultants, share information, accept advice, negotiate fairly, and act on recommendations.
- Another recurring theme is structure. Proposals, reports, workshops, interviews, negotiations, and problem solving all require clear framing and disciplined process.

### Core Frameworks to Revise

- Consulting role: reputation, network, expertise, ambiguity, client focus, and professional conduct.
- Engagement life cycle: risk assessment, winning work, contractual negotiation, delivery and project management, close-out and quality assurance.
- Proposal structure: executive summary, introduction, understanding of needs, methodology and timeline, pricing and assumptions, team CVs, and similar work.
- Audience analysis: decision makers, technical evaluators, procurement staff, end users, and broader client ecosystem.
- Trust: credibility, reliability, intimacy, and low self-orientation.
- Negotiation: interests, constraints, bottom lines, open-book versus hardball styles, scope, price, timelines, and relationship impact.
- Professional advice: evidence, options, trade-offs, recommendations, assumptions, limits, and actionability.
- Report writing: client question, executive summary, approach, findings, recommendations, roadmap, and appendices.
- Workshops and interviews: facilitation, questioning, shared understanding, stakeholder evidence, and write-up.
- Hypothesis-driven consulting: provisional answer, issue structure, evidence testing, and revision.
- Problem solving: problem definition, criteria, alternatives, analysis, decision, and action plan.
- Conflict and close-out: difficult conversations, governance, sign-off, handover, residual risks, and lessons learned.

### Exam Preparation

- Practise applying frameworks to short client scenarios rather than memorising definitions only.
- For any scenario, identify the client problem, stakeholders, evidence, risks, options, recommendation, and communication approach.
- Use course language precisely: proposal, engagement, scope, assumption, deliverable, stakeholder, sponsor, advice, risk, finding, recommendation.
- When asked to recommend, make the recommendation explicit and support it with reasoning.
- When asked to critique, identify what is missing, risky, unclear, or misaligned with the client need.

## Examples

- A proposal scenario can be answered by combining audience analysis, bid/no-bid judgement, risk assessment, and proposal structure.
- A delivery problem scenario can be answered by combining lifecycle stage, stakeholder management, communication, negotiation, and governance.
- A recommendation question can be answered by combining problem definition, criteria, evidence, options, trade-offs, and action plan.
